## The phenomenon (one line)
Give a competitive game skill-based matchmaking (pair players by current Elo rating) and the win rate stops measuring skill: because you are matched against opponents of your own rating, your rating climbs or falls until your expected score is exactly 0.5, so a top-decile player and a bottom-decile player both hover near a 50% win rate — the observed win-rate gap between the best and worst skill deciles collapses from ~0.72 under random matching to ~0.04 under SBMM (an ~17× compression) — even though the rating those same games compute still ranks true skill with correlation ~0.98.

## The folk belief
"A high win rate means you're good — win rate is the honest competitive scoreboard, the number that separates strong players from weak ones. If someone sustains a 55–60% win rate they must be more skilled; if matchmaking is working, better players should win more of their games." Win rate is treated (by players, by streamers comparing accounts, and by designers grading matchmade bots/agents) as a monotone, informative readout of skill.

## The game-design thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
Skill-based matchmaking has one job: pair you against opponents near your own rating. Under the standard Elo model your expected score against an opponent of rating R_opp is σ((R−R_opp)/S) with S=400 (log-10 logistic); when R≈R_opp that expected score is σ(0)=0.5. The Elo update is a stochastic-approximation loop that drives your rating to the fixed point where your realized score matches your expected score, i.e. to the rating at which you win ~half your (rating-matched) games. Therefore **at equilibrium every player, regardless of true skill, has an expected win rate of ~0.5** — skill is expressed as your equilibrium *rating*, not as a win-rate surplus. Two consequences the folk belief misses. (1) **Win-rate compression / the mirage:** the win rate is squeezed toward 0.5 for everyone, so it carries little magnitude information about skill — the best and worst deciles win nearly the same fraction. What residual monotonicity survives is a finite-population tail edge effect (the very top players can only be matched *down*, the very bottom only *up*), which is why the SBMM win-rate correlation floors around ~0.5 rather than exactly 0, but the *magnitude* — the decile gap — is crushed by more than an order of magnitude. (2) **The information is not lost, it is relocated:** the identical games' *rating* recovers true skill with correlation ~0.98, because the rating is exactly the sufficient statistic the matchmaker is solving for. The counterintuitive core, isolated by a same-seed control: run the SAME players with the SAME skills under RANDOM matching and their win rate becomes a near-perfect skill signal (decile gap ~0.72, corr ~0.98) — so it is the *matchmaking*, not the skill model or the sim, that turns win rate into a mirage. The honest reading: under SBMM, win rate ≈ 0.5 is the *expected* signature of a working system, a win-rate surplus mostly means you have not finished climbing to your equilibrium rating (transient disequilibrium), and the number that actually ranks skill is the rating — so grade players, matchmade bots, and competitive agents by rating (or rating velocity), never by win rate.

## The formal model (committed constants — sim-lab must reproduce exactly)
- N_PLAYERS=200 players with true latent skills drawn iid Normal(0, SKILL_SD=250) on the Elo 400-logistic scale; all start at rating R0=1500.
- Game outcome model (TRUE skill): P(a beats b) = 1/(1 + 10^{−(skill_a−skill_b)/400}).
- **SBMM regime:** for GAMES=600 rounds, each round jitter every rating by U(−15,15) Elo (a rotating near-rating pool), sort by jittered rating, pair adjacent players (rating-nearest-neighbour matching), draw each game from the true-skill model, and update both ratings with the symmetric Elo rule R_a += K·(S_a − E_a), R_b += K·((1−S_a) − (1−E_a)), K=16, where E_a = 1/(1+10^{−(R_a−R_b)/400}) is the expected score from current RATINGS. Win rate is counted only over rounds after BURN_IN=400 (the converged regime); final rating is read at the end.
- **RANDOM-matching control:** on the SAME players/skills, for RANDOM_GAMES=200 rounds shuffle all players and pair adjacent (uniform random opponents, no matchmaking, no rating), drawing outcomes from the same true-skill model; measure each player's win rate.
- Per replication record: corr(skill, final rating), corr(skill, SBMM win rate), corr(skill, random win rate), the SBMM top-vs-bottom skill-decile win-rate gap, and the random-matching decile gap (decile = top/bottom 20 players by true skill).
- A scenario's mean and standard error are taken over TRIALS=200 independent replications, se = sample-sd / √TRIALS (the P104/P105/P106/P107 /se convention: z on the estimated mean).

## Why it matters (game design)
Win rate is the most-cited competitive scoreboard in matchmade games (ranked ladders, MMR queues, auto-battlers, MOBA/shooter arenas) and the metric designers reach for to grade a matchmade *bot* or a competitive *agent* ("its win rate is 58%, it's strong"). This proposal shows that under working skill-based matchmaking the win rate is a mirage: it is compressed toward a skill-independent ~50% fixed point (decile gap ~0.04 vs ~0.72 under random matching), so it massively *understates* skill differences and a sustained surplus mostly signals transient disequilibrium (still climbing) rather than durable strength — while the rating the same games compute recovers skill almost perfectly. Decision rule for a designer/operator: never rank players, tune a bot, or A/B a competitive agent by win rate under SBMM — use the rating (or rating velocity toward equilibrium); expect a flat win rate across skill as the *signature of correct* matchmaking, not as evidence that skill is irrelevant; and if you must publish a skill-legible scoreboard, publish the rating, because win rate under matchmaking is designed to be ~50% for everyone. The same lens audits any matchmade adversarial loop where a controller equalizes opponents: the win/loss column goes flat by construction, and the informative statistic is the latent rating the matcher is solving for.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The exact 0.5 expected-score fixed point rests on the standard Elo/Bradley–Terry logistic win model and a matchmaker that pairs on rating; the QUALITATIVE claims — win-rate compression toward 0.5 under rating-matching, and skill recovery by the rating — hold for ANY monotone win-probability model and ANY rating system that converges to the sufficient statistic, because the compression is a fixed-point property of "match on the estimate, update the estimate to fit outcomes," not an artifact of the logistic. The result does depend on structural choices, all standard: (a) matchmaking is on rating (the definition of SBMM); (b) a symmetric zero-sum Elo update with a single K (larger K speeds convergence and slightly widens residual win-rate noise, smaller K narrows it — neither reverses the direction); (c) enough games past burn-in for ratings to converge (BURN_IN=400 with K=16; a season too short would leave ratings far from equilibrium and INFLATE corr(skill,winrate) — measured: the shorter the convergence, the *less* compression, so the pinned world is conservative for the mirage, not tuned to inflate it); (d) the residual corr(skill,SBMM-winrate)~0.5 is a finite-population tail edge effect (extreme players match only inward), which is why the headline is the compressed decile GAP and the corr CONTRAST, not a claim that win rate is literally uncorrelated with skill. The random-matching control (G2) is the load-bearing guard: it demonstrates on the identical skill model and estimator that win rate WOULD carry the full skill signal (corr 0.98, gap 0.72) — so the compression is caused by matchmaking, not by the sim. The claim is scoped: under rating-based matchmaking with a convergent Elo rating and a logistic skill model, the win rate is compressed to a near-skill-independent fixed point while the rating recovers skill — demonstrated on the pinned constants, mechanism-explained (the σ(0)=0.5 Elo fixed point), not asserted as a universal law.
