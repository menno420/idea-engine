## The phenomenon (one line)
When a guild reward needs only ONE member to pay a cost (soak the mechanic, farm the reagent, staff the event, hit a shared contribution bar), each rational member free-rides harder as the roster grows, so the equilibrium probability that NOBODY volunteers **rises with group size** — from 25.0% at N=2 to 48.1% at N=20 on the pinned world — even though the reward, the cost, and every member's willingness are unchanged; the total expected effort stays pinned near **−ln(c/b)** regardless of N.

## The folk belief
"A bigger guild is a safer guild. If a raid needs someone to soak the beam or someone to go farm the flask, then the more people we have, the more likely SOMEBODY steps up — with 20 people online, of course one of them will do it. Recruiting more members can only help the odds that the chore gets covered; a big active roster is insurance against nobody doing the boring job." The instinct reads the group as a pile of independent chances: 20 members each with some willingness to volunteer must beat 2 members, because there are ten times as many shots at "someone does it." It feels like the probability of coverage can only go up with N.

## The game-design thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The intuition treats each member's willingness as FIXED and independent, but willingness is STRATEGIC: a member volunteers only if the expected payoff from volunteering beats the expected payoff from waiting for someone else. The good is a binary public good — one volunteer suffices, and every member equally enjoys the benefit b whether or not they were the one who paid c. So each member faces a wait-or-act gamble whose value depends on how likely SOMEONE ELSE acts, and in a symmetric group everyone reasons identically, giving a symmetric mixed-strategy equilibrium. A member is indifferent between:

    volunteer:  payoff = b − c          (provision guaranteed, you pay the cost)
    free-ride:  payoff = b · P(≥1 of the other N−1 volunteers) = b·(1 − (1−p)^{N−1})

Setting them equal pins the equilibrium volunteer probability p*:

    b − c = b·(1 − (1−p)^{N−1})   ⇒   (1−p)^{N−1} = c/b   ⇒   p*(N) = 1 − (c/b)^{1/(N−1)}

and the probability the good is NOT provided (nobody volunteers) is

    P_fail(N) = (1 − p*)^N = (c/b)^{N/(N−1)}.

Because the exponent N/(N−1) DECREASES toward 1 as N grows and c/b<1, the term (c/b)^{N/(N−1)} **increases** toward c/b. Two consequences the folk belief misses. **(1) More members RAISES the chance nobody acts.** At c/b=0.5, P_fail runs 0.25 (N=2) → 0.42 (N=5) → 0.46 (N=10) → 0.48 (N=20), climbing monotonically toward the ceiling c/b=0.5 — the bystander effect (Darley & Latané 1968) is not apathy or bad culture, it is the symmetric equilibrium of self-interested waiting. **(2) The total effort does not scale with the roster.** The expected number of volunteers is N·p*(N) = N(1−(c/b)^{1/(N−1)}), which is essentially FLAT in N and converges to −ln(c/b)=0.693 — so twenty members supply the same ~0.7 expected volunteers as a well-tuned pair; the extra eighteen members buy pure dilution of responsibility, not extra contribution. The honest reading for a designer: a "someone will do it" mechanic does not get safer with a bigger guild — it gets RISKIER, and the cure is never "recruit more" but "remove the substitutability" (assign the duty to a named role, or pay the volunteer a private surplus so volunteering dominates).

## The formal model (committed constants — sim-lab must reproduce exactly)
- N members; the reward is provided iff at least one member volunteers.
- Each volunteer pays a private cost c; every member (volunteer or not) enjoys benefit b if the good is provided, 0 otherwise; 0<c<b.
- **Symmetric mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium:** each member volunteers independently with probability p*(N)=1−(c/b)^{1/(N−1)} (the indifference FOC above).
- **Provision failure (per trial):** fail = 1 iff no member volunteers, else 0. Equilibrium expectation P_fail(N)=(c/b)^{N/(N−1)}.
- **Fixed-rate CONTROL (specificity):** every member volunteers at the SMALL-GROUP rate p0=p*(N_LO) regardless of N (non-re-optimizing members — the folk-belief model where willingness is a fixed constant). Its failure rate (1−p0)^N DECREASES in N, so the control FLIPS the sign of the size→failure relationship; the rise is caused by strategic re-optimization (dilution), not by group size per se.
- **Paired common random numbers:** within a trial one shared uniform vector u (length N_MAX) drives every N and both the strategic and the fixed-rate control measurements — member i volunteers at level N iff u_i < p*(N) (and iff u_i < p0 for the control) — so the group sizes are coupled and the paired dose difference fail[N_HI]−fail[N_LO] is a low-variance paired statistic. ## Why it matters (game design)
Guilds, raids, and any persistent multiplayer group lean on "someone will do it" mechanics constantly — a raid needs a player to soak a one-shot beam, a guild bank needs someone to restock reagents, a community event needs a volunteer host, a guild-perk contribution bar needs one member to top it off. Designers treat a bigger, more active roster as insurance, but this proposal shows the opposite: for any duty where ONE contributor suffices and the rest can free-ride, the equilibrium probability that nobody acts RISES with the group size (toward c/b), and the total expected contribution stays pinned near −ln(c/b) no matter how many members are online. Two harms follow. (1) The reliability of shared duties DEGRADES as a guild grows — exactly when leadership assumes it is most robust — producing the familiar "big guild, nobody restocks the bank / nobody signs up to lead" pathology, which reads as a culture problem but is a mechanism-design one. (2) It silently punishes growth: recruiting members can lower the coverage rate of collective chores, so a guild's operational health does not track its size. The fixes are structural, not motivational. Decision rule for a designer/operator: for any "one contributor suffices" duty, do NOT rely on roster size — either ASSIGN the duty to a named role (a designated soaker / banker / raid lead turns the mixed game into a dominant-strategy pure one, restoring provision to ~1), pay the volunteer a private surplus that makes volunteering dominant (an internalized b−c, the reward-share cure), or convert the substitutable good into an individually-rewarded task so contributions add instead of substitute. Measure the pivotal-cost ratio c/b of your chore and read (c/b)^{N/(N−1)} as the coverage-failure floor your current design accepts.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The result rests on standard structural choices, all pinned. The two load-bearing assumptions are: (a) **symmetric players with a common (b,c)** — every member has the same benefit and cost, giving the clean symmetric mixed equilibrium p*(N)=1−(c/b)^{1/(N−1)}; asymmetric costs shift WHO carries the load but keep the qualitative bystander result (a larger substitutable set still dilutes each member's incentive), and in the fully asymmetric limit the lowest-cost member is likeliest to act — the aggregate can only lose reliability as substitutes are added; (b) **one contributor suffices (threshold k=1)** — the pure volunteer's dilemma; a k-of-N threshold shifts the failure law and can introduce an interior optimal squad size, a distinct object flagged as a follow-up. Further pinned choices: (c) a one-shot simultaneous game (no communication / no assignment) — the whole point is that ABSENT a coordinating institution the symmetric equilibrium is where self-interested play lands; adding assignment or a binding volunteer is exactly the modeled cure; (d) independent mixing across members; (e) b=1, c=0.5 (c/b=0.5), N up to 20. The QUALITATIVE claim — for a binary public good with one pivotal contributor and substitutable effort, the symmetric-equilibrium probability that nobody acts rises with N toward c/b while total expected effort stays near −ln(c/b) — holds for any 0<c<b and any N≥2; the b=1/c=0.5 form just fixes the magnitudes and makes the seed reproducible. The claim is scoped: under symmetric players, a one-of-N binary public good, and simultaneous mixed play, P_fail(N)=(c/b)^{N/(N−1)} rises from 25.0% at N=2 to 48.1% at N=20 — demonstrated on the pinned constants, mechanism-explained (the volunteer indifference FOC), not asserted as a universal law of all group behavior.
