## The phenomenon (one line)
A pity timer that guarantees a reward on the K-th consecutive miss looks strictly kinder as K shrinks — fewer brutal dry streaks — but player retention is NON-monotone in K: it peaks at an interior K*, because a tight ceiling makes the reward arrive on schedule (predictable, "earned not surprising") and the variable-ratio anticipation that keeps players pulling collapses, while a loose ceiling lets long droughts drive them off in frustration. The kindest-looking pity is not the best pity.

## The folk belief
"A tighter pity timer is strictly better for the player — it caps the worst-case dry streak, so lower K is always kinder and always retains more." Retention is modeled as monotone decreasing in the worst-case wait; the reward-timing UNPREDICTABILITY that a tight ceiling destroys is not treated as a decision variable. Under that model the answer is always the tightest ceiling the economy can afford.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
A "cycle" is the run of pulls up to and including the next reward. Each pull hits naturally with probability p; if K−1 consecutive misses occur, the reward is FORCED on pull K. So the cycle length is L = min(G, K) where G ~ Geometric(p) on {1,2,...} (P(G=k) = (1−p)^(k−1)·p), and **L == K exactly when the reward was the forced pity-ceiling reward** — the predictable, "unearned" one. The fraction of rewards that arrive via the ceiling is P(L=K) = (1−p)^(K−1), which FALLS geometrically as K loosens.

The player runs cycles until they quit. Two churn hazards act per cycle, each ENDOGENOUS to the realized process (not an assumed function of K):
- **Frustration** h_frust(L) = a·max(0, L − L0): a long realized drought past the tolerance L0 drives a quit. Because L ≤ K, a tight ceiling bounds the worst drought; E[h_frust](K) = a·E[max(0, L − L0)] RISES with K (fatter tail).
- **Boredom** h_bore(L) = c·1[L == K]: the reward was the predictable forced one — the variable-ratio schedule has collapsed to a fixed ceiling, so the pull carries no anticipation. E[h_bore](K) = c·(1−p)^(K−1) FALLS with K.

Per cycle the player quits with probability h(L) = min(1, h_frust(L) + h_bore(L)); retention = the number of completed reward-cycles before the quit.

The trap is the sum of a rising term and a geometrically-falling term. The exact per-cycle expected quit hazard
  E[h_total](K) = a·E[max(0, L − L0)] + c·(1−p)^(K−1)
is strictly UNIMODAL with a unique interior minimum, so retention (≈ inverse hazard) PEAKS at an interior K*. A tight ceiling: near-zero frustration, but most rewards are forced/predictable (boredom high). A loose ceiling: maximally surprising schedule (boredom low), but droughts get long (frustration high). Neither extreme is best.

A clean attribution falls out of the pinned world: **for every K ≤ L0, no realized drought can exceed the tolerance** (L ≤ K ≤ L0 ⇒ max(0, L−L0) = 0), so h_frust ≡ 0 EXACTLY across the whole tight arm. The entire retention climb from the tightest ceiling up to the peak is therefore caused PURELY by the falling boredom hazard — zero frustration confound. Past the peak, boredom is small and the rising frustration term takes over. Remove boredom (c=0) and tightening is never penalized → the optimum slides to the tightest ceiling. Remove frustration (a=0) and loosening is never penalized → the optimum slides to the loosest. Both hazards are necessary for the interior optimum.

## Why it matters (game-ops)
The pity ceiling is a single integer every gacha/loot economy ships, and the default heuristic — "make it as tight as the economy can bear, it's the kind thing" — is exactly the dominated choice. The model says: tune the ceiling to the INTERIOR retention optimum, not the tightest; the kindest-looking pity churns players two ways (it makes rewards predictable, and predictable rewards stop pulling). It also predicts a diagnostic signature a live game can watch: when a large share of rewards arrive EXACTLY at the ceiling (L==K), the pity is over-tightened — the schedule has collapsed to a metronome and the variable-ratio engagement is gone. The actionable rule: set the ceiling where the forced-reward fraction (1−p)^(K−1) is small enough to preserve surprise but the drought tail is short enough to hold frustration down — an interior point the "tighter is always kinder" heuristic cannot see.
