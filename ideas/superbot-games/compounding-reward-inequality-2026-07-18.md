## The phenomenon (one line)
Give a game economy a compounding percentage reward — each day every player's holdings change by a random factor drawn from the SAME distribution with a small positive average (no skill, no choice, no edge) — and let it stack on the running total; after a season of DAYS days the wealth distribution is log-normal and the wealth concentrates violently among identical players: the top-decile wealth share climbs to ~0.55 and the Gini to ~0.68, while the SAME per-day percentages paid on the starting stake (not compounded) leave everyone near the fair ~0.15 top-decile share.

## The folk belief
"If the daily bonus is the same random draw for everybody and nobody has an edge, the economy stays roughly egalitarian — a few players get lucky streaks and a few get unlucky ones, but it all averages out and the spread stays modest. A fair reward can't manufacture a rich-vs-poor gap out of identical players." Designers reach for percentage-based / compounding rewards (interest mechanics, multiplicative loot multipliers, "+X% of your holdings" daily logins, idle-game prestige multipliers) precisely because they feel fair and self-balancing.

## The game-design thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The intuition confuses ADDITIVE fairness with MULTIPLICATIVE fairness. When a reward COMPOUNDS, terminal wealth is a PRODUCT of daily factors, so its logarithm is a SUM of iid daily log-returns and terminal wealth is **log-normal** (Gibrat's law / multiplicative stochastic processes). Concretely, if each day's log-return is g_d ~ Normal(μ, σ), then after DAYS days

    W = W0 · ∏_d exp(g_d) = W0 · exp(S),   S = Σ_d g_d ~ Normal(DAYS·μ, DAYS·σ²)  (exact),

so terminal wealth is log-normal with **terminal log-sd σ_T = σ·√DAYS**. Two consequences the folk belief misses. **(1) Concentration is scale-free in σ_T and grows without bound.** For a log-normal the Gini is exactly `2·Φ(σ_T/√2) − 1`, the top-p wealth share is exactly `Φ(σ_T − Φ⁻¹(1−p))`, and the median-to-mean ratio is exactly `exp(−σ_T²/2)` — all functions of σ_T alone, independent of the drift μ. As the season lengthens σ_T = σ·√DAYS grows, so the top decile's share rises toward 1 and the typical (median) player's wealth falls further and further below the mean, entirely from the accumulation of iid shocks — no skill, no preferential matching, no rich-player advantage in the rules. A lucky early streak gets multiplied by every later factor, so early noise is amplified permanently: the process has memory through the running total. **(2) The inequality is caused by COMPOUNDING, not by the luck.** Take the SAME daily log-returns and pay them on the STARTING stake instead of the running total — W = W0·(1 + Σ_d g_d) — and terminal wealth is `W0·(1 + Normal(DAYS·μ, DAYS·σ²))`, a SYMMETRIC distribution whose coefficient of variation SHRINKS with the (positive) drift, so the concentration collapses back toward the fair share. Same randomness, same players, same per-day percentages — remove the compounding and the runaway inequality disappears. The honest reading for a designer: a percentage/compounding reward is a rich-get-richer engine by construction; a flat leaderboard is NOT the expected outcome of a "fair" compounding bonus, it is the outcome only of a NON-compounding one — so if the design goal is an egalitarian economy, pay rewards on a fixed base (additive), cap the compounding horizon, or rebase periodically; and never read a widening wealth gap under a compounding reward as evidence that some players are more skilled.

## Why it matters (game design)
Percentage-based and compounding rewards are everywhere in game economies — interest/savings mechanics, multiplicative loot and XP multipliers, "+X% of your holdings" daily logins, idle-game prestige multipliers, staking yields. This proposal shows that any such reward is a rich-get-richer engine by construction: even with IDENTICAL players, ZERO skill, and a perfectly fair per-day draw, compounding drives the wealth distribution to log-normal and concentrates most of the economy in a vanishing fraction of players (top decile ~55%, Gini ~0.68 on the pinned world), while the median player falls to ~37% of the average. The same-shocks additive control is the load-bearing guard: pay the identical daily percentages on a fixed base instead of the running total and the concentration collapses to the fair ~0.15 — so the inequality is the compounding, not the luck or any player advantage. Decision rule for a designer/operator: if the goal is an egalitarian or bounded-spread economy, do NOT use an uncapped compounding reward — pay on a fixed base (additive), cap the compounding horizon, rebase/normalize periodically, or add a wealth-proportional sink; expect (and communicate) that a widening wealth gap under a compounding reward is the DESIGNED outcome of the reward structure, not evidence that some players are better; and never grade players, tune a bot economy, or A/B a reward by the resulting wealth spread under compounding, because the spread is manufactured by the multiplicative process regardless of skill.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The exact log-normal terminal distribution rests on iid Normal daily log-returns (so S = Σg is exactly Normal and W=W0·exp(S) is exactly log-normal), which makes the Gini `2Φ(σ_T/√2)−1`, top-p share `Φ(σ_T−Φ⁻¹(1−p))`, and median/mean `exp(−σ_T²/2)` exact. The QUALITATIVE claims — compounding an iid multiplicative reward produces a right-skewed, concentrating wealth distribution whose dispersion grows with the horizon, while paying the same shocks additively does not — hold for ANY iid multiplicative factor with finite log-variance (the CLT drives log-wealth to Normal, so terminal wealth is asymptotically log-normal regardless of the per-day factor's shape); the Normal-log-return choice just makes the anchors exact and the seed reproducible. The result depends on standard structural choices, all pinned: (a) the reward compounds on the running total (the definition of a percentage/multiplicative reward); (b) the daily draws are iid and idiosyncratic across players (a COMMON daily shock would move everyone together and preserve equality — the concentration needs idiosyncratic multiplicative variance, which is exactly what a per-player random bonus is); (c) enough periods for the dispersion to matter (σ_T = σ·√DAYS grows with the horizon; a very short season shows little concentration — the pinned 200-day horizon gives σ_T=1.41, and a LONGER season would show MORE concentration, so the constants are conservative for the claim, not tuned to inflate it); (d) the additive control is the same shocks paid on the fixed base, which is the precise non-compounding counterfactual, not a different reward. The claim is scoped: under an iid compounding percentage reward across identical players, terminal wealth is log-normal and concentrates (Gini→1, median/mean→0 as the horizon grows) while the same-shocks additive reward stays near the fair share — demonstrated on the pinned constants, mechanism-explained (Gibrat / multiplicative log-normality), not asserted as a universal law.
