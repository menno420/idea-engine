## The phenomenon (one line)
A catch-up rubber-band that boosts the trailing racer in proportion to the leader's gap looks strictly kinder — and strictly closer — as the gain k rises, but engagement is NON-monotone in k: it peaks at an interior k*, because a weak band lets the intrinsically faster racer open a persistent lead (predictable, decided-early boredom), while past the exact stability boundary k=2 the feedback loop becomes UNSTABLE — the gap oscillates with diverging amplitude, lead-changes become a wild catapult, and the winner is decided by the controller's instability rather than skill (a coin-flip that churns the skilled). The strongest-looking rubber-band is not the best rubber-band.

## The folk belief
"A stronger catch-up boost is strictly better — it keeps the pack together, so a higher gain always makes the race closer and always more exciting." Closeness is modeled as monotone in the gain (the steady-state lead g* = F/k shrinks as k rises, which is true and visible), and the loop's DYNAMIC stability — whether the gap converges, oscillates, or diverges — is not treated as a decision variable. Under that model the answer is always the strongest boost the game can ship.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
Model the signed position gap g(t) = x_A − x_B between an intrinsically faster racer A and a rubber-banded racer B. Absent correction, A's skill edge opens the gap at a constant rate F = 2s per tick. The rubber-band boosts whoever trails in proportion to the gap it measured d ticks ago (sensor/telemetry delay is unavoidable in any real engine). The gap obeys a linear delay-difference equation — the textbook form of a discrete-time proportional controller:

    g(t+1) = g(t) + F − k·g(t−d) + ε(t),   g(τ) = g0 for τ ≤ 0,   ε ~ Normal(0, σ²)

**The folk-visible benefit is real but partial.** The steady-state mean lead is g* = F/k: a stronger boost does shrink the deserving racer's lead (closer race). That is the whole of what the "stronger is closer" intuition sees.

**The hidden cost is dynamic.** The homogeneous loop g(t+1) = (1−k)·g(t) [for d=0] has pole (1−k), so:
- **0 < k < 1 (over-damped):** the gap approaches g* monotonically — the leader always leads, the follower never overtakes → few/no lead-changes → predictable, decided-early.
- **1 < k < 2 (under-damped):** the pole is negative, the approach oscillates — the follower overshoots into the lead and back → genuine lead-changes, and per-tick noise σ excites the lightly-damped mode so the gap swings around g* with AMPLIFIED variance Var(g) = σ²/(k(2−k)) (the controller's noise-sensitivity function, which rises toward the boundary). This is the exciting regime.
- **k ≥ 2 (unstable):** |1−k| ≥ 1 — the loop no longer contracts; the gap oscillates with diverging amplitude, position is dominated by the instability, and the winner at the line is a near-coin-flip decorrelated from skill.

Two engagement hazards fall straight out, one per arm:
- **decided-early (boredom)** at low k: the faster racer runs away; lead-changes ≈ 0; the outcome is known long before the finish.
- **no-agency (instability churn)** at high k: past k=2 the gap variance diverges and the outcome stops tracking skill — the skilled player loses to the catapult and churns ("my skill doesn't matter").

Score engagement as E(k) = (lead-change rate) · (2·skill-fidelity − 1), where lead-change rate is the per-tick zero-crossing frequency of g (the excitement) and skill-fidelity is the fraction of time the deserving racer A actually leads (→1 when the outcome tracks skill, →½ when it is a coin-flip). E is a RISING excitement term times a FALLING skill-fidelity term, so it is unimodal with an interior maximum: at low k excitement ≈ 0 (E ≈ 0); past k=2 skill-fidelity → ½ so (2·fid−1) → 0 AND the loop diverges (E → 0). Neither extreme is best; the optimum is an interior gain just below the instability boundary.

The sensor delay makes the boundary WORSE, exactly as control theory predicts. For d=1 the loop is g(t+1) = g(t) − k·g(t−1); its characteristic z² − z + k = 0 is Jury-stable iff 0 < k < 1 — the delay HALVES the instability threshold from 2 to 1. Delay is not cosmetic; it is a real tightening of the safe design band.

## Why it matters (game-ops)
The catch-up boost is a single gain every racer/party/PvP economy ships (Mario-Kart rubber-band, MOBA comeback-gold, shooter skill-based leash, any "keep it close" dial), and the default heuristic — "crank it up, closeness is good" — is exactly the dominated choice, because it optimizes the visible steady-state lead g* = F/k while ignoring the loop's stability. The model says: set the gain to the interior engagement optimum just BELOW the stability boundary, not as strong as the economy can bear. It also hands a diagnostic a live game can watch: when the gap's tick-to-tick variance starts rising super-linearly with the gain (the noise-sensitivity σ²/(k(2−k)) blowing up), the rubber-band is approaching instability — the excitement has become a catapult, and the outcome is decoupling from skill. And it warns that any added SENSOR DELAY (server tick, telemetry averaging) silently lowers the safe gain, so a band that felt great at 30 Hz can go unstable when the same gain runs on a laggier loop.
