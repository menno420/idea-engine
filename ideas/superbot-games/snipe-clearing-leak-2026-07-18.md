## The phenomenon (one line)
A hard-close game auction settles at a second price computed over only the bids that TRANSMIT before the deadline, and last-instant snipes fail to transmit with rate q, so the effective field is thinned and the winner pays the second-highest of a random subset — a clear that at q=0.15 sits **5.02% below** the full-field competitive second price v_(2) that a soft-close (auto-extend) auction would realize, a silent seller→best-connection value transfer (plus an AH-cut gold-sink) that **grows with q** (1.51% at q=0.05 → 9.45% at q=0.25).

## The folk belief
"The auto-extend / anti-snipe timer is a courtesy — it's there so a player who bid honestly doesn't get robbed by someone clicking at 0.2 seconds left. It's a fairness/UX nicety about WHO wins, not about the PRICE. A hard deadline and a soft deadline sell the item to the same top bidder for about the same money; the timer just changes the feel, smooths out the salt, and keeps people from rage-quitting. It has no effect on the house's revenue or the economy's gold sink." Anti-snipe is reached for precisely because it feels like politeness — eBay-style hard closes breed sniping, and a soft close is sold to players as "no more losing to snipers," which invites reading the timer as social lubricant rather than a market-microstructure lever.

## The game-design thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The intuition confuses WHO wins with WHAT the winner pays. Under a second-price (or ascending-proxy) clear the seller's revenue is the SECOND-highest valuation among the bidders whose bids actually count, so the pricing depends entirely on how many of the K bidders' bids are IN the pool at the moment of the clear. A hard close forces the late bids into a single last-instant transmission that each fails with probability q (tick granularity, latency, rate limits, a dropped packet). Losing a bid rarely changes the WINNER — the top valuation usually still transmits — but it routinely changes the SECOND-highest transmitted valuation downward, and the second price is exactly the revenue. So the hard-close revenue is the second-highest of a q-thinned subset, and

    P_hard = second_highest({ v_i : bid i transmits }),   each i transmits w.p. (1−q),
    P_soft = v_(2) = second_highest(v_1..v_K)  (the extension lets everyone respond),
    leak   = P_soft − P_hard ≥ 0,   E[leak] ↑ in q.

A closed form pins it. With m of K bids transmitting (m ~ Binomial(K, 1−q)) and valuations iid Uniform(0,1), the expected second-highest of the transmitted subset is E[v_(m−1:m)] = (m−1)/(m+1) for m≥2 and 0 for m<2 (reserve), so

    E[P_hard](q) = Σ_{m=2}^{K} C(K,m)(1−q)^m q^{K−m} · (m−1)/(m+1),   E[P_soft] = (K−1)/(K+1),

and E[leak](q) = E[P_soft] − E[P_hard](q), monotone increasing from 0 at q=0. Two consequences the folk belief misses. **(1) The timer sets the PRICE, not just the winner.** At K=8, q=0.15 the leak is 0.039 on a 0.777 clear — 5.02% of revenue silently transferred from the seller (and the AH's percentage cut) to whoever's bid did land, i.e. the best-connected bidder; at q=0.25 it is 9.45%. This is a gold-sink leak: the AH cut is levied on a depressed price, so the sink under-drains exactly when latency is worst. **(2) It scales with the friction, and vanishes without it.** At q=0 the transmitted set is the full field and hard = soft EXACTLY (leak ≡ 0, every auction), so the effect is caused by the transmission friction, not by the auction format per se — the anti-snipe extension's economic job is to remove q from the pricing path. The honest reading for a designer: an auto-extend timer is not politeness, it is a revenue-integrity and gold-sink control; price it as one, and expect the leak (and the value transfer to low-latency clients) to grow with server tick, latency, and rate-limit friction q.

## The formal model (committed constants — sim-lab must reproduce exactly)
- K = 8 bidders; private valuations iid Uniform(0,1) (drawn once per auction, common to both formats — paired).
- **Soft close (anti-snipe / ascending English):** the auto-extend timer lets the full field respond, so the clearing price is the second-highest of ALL K valuations, v_(2).
- **Hard close (sniping):** each bidder submits one last-instant sealed bid that transmits with probability (1−q); the late-bid / server-tick transmission-FAILURE rate is q. The clearing price is the second-price over the TRANSMITTED subset — the second-highest transmitted valuation, or 0 (reserve) if fewer than 2 bids transmit.
- **Leak (per auction)** = P_soft − P_hard ≥ 0.
- q levels: q ∈ {0.0, 0.05, 0.15, 0.25}, with 0.0 the placebo/specificity level, Q_LO=0.05, Q_STAR=0.15, Q_HI=0.25.
- **Paired common random numbers:** within a trial the SAME valuation draws and the SAME per-bidder transmission draws (a uniform u_i compared to q) feed every q level, so the q-levels are coupled and the dose-response contrast leak(Q_HI)−leak(Q_LO) is a low-variance paired difference.
- **Closed-form anchor:** E[P_hard](q) = Σ_{m=2}^{K} C(K,m)(1−q)^m q^{K−m}·(m−1)/(m+1) (binomial mixture over the number m of transmitted bids); E[P_soft] = (K−1)/(K+1). Gate z-scores use se = std/√TRIALS on the estimated mean.

## Why it matters (game design)
Auction houses are a core economic organ of live games (WoW-style AH, marketplace/trading-post systems, and any deadline-cleared bid market), and their clearing rule is usually treated as plumbing while the anti-snipe timer is treated as a comfort feature. This proposal shows the timer is a REVENUE and GOLD-SINK integrity mechanism: a hard close prices the item at the second-highest bid that transmits, and last-instant snipes drop with rate q, so the clear leaks below the true competitive second price v_(2) — 5.02% at q=0.15, 9.45% at q=0.25, scaling with server tick / latency / rate-limit friction. Two harms follow. (1) A silent value transfer from the seller (and the AH's percentage cut) to the best-connected bidder — low-latency clients systematically win at depressed prices, a pay-to-connect advantage the UI never shows. (2) A gold-sink leak: the AH cut is a primary currency drain, and it under-drains exactly when the clear is depressed, weakening inflation control precisely under load. The fix is the soft close: auto-extend on any late action so the full field responds and the clear returns to v_(2), removing q from the pricing path. Decision rule for a designer/operator: treat the anti-snipe / auto-extend timer as a market-microstructure control, not politeness — measure the transmission-failure rate q on your tick, price the leak (and the connection-quality value transfer) it removes, and prefer soft-close / proxy-ascending settlement wherever the AH cut is a load-bearing gold sink.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The result rests on standard structural choices, all pinned. The two load-bearing assumptions are: (a) **Uniform(0,1) valuations** — iid across bidders, which gives the clean order-statistic anchor E[v_(m−1:m)] = (m−1)/(m+1) and makes the closed-form E[P_hard](q) an exact binomial mixture; a different value distribution shifts the magnitudes but not the direction (thinning a sample can only lower or hold its second-highest, so leak ≥ 0 for any exchangeable valuation law); (b) **iid transmission failure** — each late bid drops independently with the same probability q (a single last-instant transmission per bidder), so the transmitted count is Binomial(K, 1−q); correlated drops (a shared server stall) would change the mixture weights but keep leak monotone in the drop rate. Further pinned choices: (c) a pure second-price / proxy-ascending clear so revenue equals the second-highest counted bid (a first-price or pay-as-bid rule changes the object); (d) the soft close is idealized as full-field response (the extension always lets every bidder react), the clean v_(2) benchmark; (e) K=8 and reserve 0. The QUALITATIVE claim — a hard-close second-price clear over q-thinned last-instant bids lands at or below the full-field second price, and the gap grows with q and vanishes at q=0 — holds for any exchangeable valuations and any independent (or positively-correlated) transmission failure; the Uniform+iid form just makes E[leak](q) an exact finite sum and the seed reproducible. The claim is scoped: under iid Uniform valuations and iid Bernoulli(q) transmission failure with a second-price clear, E[leak](q) rises from 0 at q=0 to 5.02% of the clearing price at q=0.15 and 9.45% at q=0.25 — demonstrated on the pinned constants, mechanism-explained (order statistics of a binomially-thinned sample), not asserted as a universal law.
