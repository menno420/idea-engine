## The phenomenon (one line)
A screening test with sensitivity sens = P(positive | diseased) and specificity spec = P(negative | healthy) is applied to a population with disease prevalence (base rate) prev. The probability the patient is actually diseased GIVEN a positive result — the positive predictive value — is **not** the test accuracy; by Bayes it is `PPV = (sens·prev)/(sens·prev + (1−spec)·(1−prev))`, which DEPENDS ON prev. On the pinned world (sens=spec=0.99) the exact posterior at prev=0.01 is **exactly 0.5** (positive-rate 0.0198), the simulated PPV over 39711 positives among 2,000,000 individuals is **0.497746**; drop the prevalence to prev=0.001 and the exact posterior COLLAPSES to **0.0901639**, the simulated PPV over 21835 positives is **0.092466** — a "99% accurate" positive is a coin flip at 1% prevalence and worse than 1-in-10 at 0.1%.

## The folk belief
"The test is 99% accurate, so if it comes back positive there's about a 99% chance I have the disease — accuracy tells me how much to trust a positive result. A test that is right 99 times out of 100 is right about my positive result 99 times out of 100." The intuition reads P(disease | positive) straight off the test's characteristic P(positive | disease)=sens (the base-rate-neglect / prosecutor's-fallacy confusion of a conditional with its converse), and forgets the population the test is applied to: when the disease is rare, the overwhelming majority of the people tested are healthy, so even a small per-healthy-person false-positive rate (1−spec) generates a flood of false positives that drowns the handful of true positives. Accuracy is confused with meaning.

## The probabilistic thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The intuition confuses the test's forward conditional `sens = P(positive | diseased)` with the posterior `P(diseased | positive)` it actually wants. Bayes' theorem relates them through the base rate:

    P(diseased | positive) = P(positive | diseased) P(diseased) / P(positive)
                           = (sens·prev) / (sens·prev + (1−spec)·(1−prev)).

Write the numerator (true-positive mass) as TP = sens·prev and the false-positive mass as FP = (1−spec)·(1−prev); then `PPV = TP/(TP+FP)` and the whole story is the RATIO of these two masses. Two facts the folk belief misses. **(1) The 50% knife-edge.** With a symmetric test sens=spec=q the PPV equals ½ exactly when `q·prev = (1−q)·(1−prev)`, i.e. at prevalence `prev* = 1−q`; for q=0.99 that is `prev* = 0.01`, so at 1% prevalence a positive is a literal coin flip regardless of how impressive "99% accurate" sounds. Below that prevalence the posterior is < ½ — a positive result makes disease MORE likely than the base rate, but still less likely than not. **(2) The collapse deepens as prevalence falls.** TP scales like prev while FP scales like (1−prev)≈constant, so `PPV ≈ (sens/(1−spec))·prev` for small prev — LINEAR in the base rate near zero. Halving the prevalence roughly halves the PPV: from prev=0.01 (PPV=0.5) to prev=0.001 the posterior falls to 0.0901639, and it keeps collapsing toward 0 as the condition gets rarer. The honest reading: a positive result updates you from the prior prev to the posterior PPV, and at low base rates that update lands far short of certainty — the test's accuracy caps how INFORMATIVE a positive is, but the base rate sets where the posterior actually lands. This is why mass-screening a rare condition with even an excellent test produces mostly false alarms, and why confirmatory (second, ideally independent) testing exists: a second positive multiplies the likelihood ratio again and drags the posterior back up.

## Why it matters (cross-cutting)
Any decision that reads a rare-event flag as if the flag's accuracy were its reliability inherits the base-rate collapse. Decision rule for an operator/clinician/analyst: (1) a positive result updates you from the prior prevalence to the posterior `PPV = (sens·prev)/(sens·prev+(1−spec)(1−prev))`, NOT to the test's accuracy — with a symmetric q-accurate test the posterior is only ½ at prevalence 1−q and collapses roughly LINEARLY in the base rate below that, so mass-screening a rare condition produces mostly false positives no matter how good the test; (2) to make a positive mean something at low prevalence you must either raise the pre-test probability (test a targeted, higher-prevalence subgroup instead of the whole population) or confirm with a second, ideally independent test (each independent positive multiplies the likelihood ratio `sens/(1−spec)` again, dragging the posterior back up); (3) this is base-rate neglect / the false-positive paradox / the prosecutor's fallacy — it governs medical screening, fraud/anomaly/intrusion-detection alerting, spam and content-moderation flags, A/B-test "significant" winners under many comparisons, and any rare-class classifier: a 99%-accurate rare-event alarm is mostly false alarms, and an alerting budget sized as if accuracy were precision silently drowns operators in false positives. Report precision (=PPV) at the DEPLOYED base rate, never the raw accuracy.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The anchor `PPV = sens·prev/(sens·prev+(1−spec)(1−prev))` is Bayes' theorem for a binary test under a Bernoulli disease indicator — no distributional assumption beyond (i) i.i.d. individuals at a fixed base rate and (ii) test errors independent of everything given disease status, both of which the model pins exactly (`rng.random()` draws). The 50%-knee `prev* = 1−q` and the linear-in-prev small-prevalence collapse `PPV ≈ (sens/(1−spec))·prev` are algebraic identities, not fitted curves; the constants merely make the anchor exact and the seed reproducible. Scoping: real screening has correlated errors (a diseased subpopulation the test systematically misses raises FN), verification/confirmation bias, and imperfect independence between repeat tests — all of which the i.i.d. conditional-independence model omits; but every such departure only makes a single positive LESS informative than the clean Bayes PPV (dependence between the disease indicator and the error process cannot raise PPV above the independent-error value for fixed marginal sens/spec), so the pinned world is the best case and the collapse is if anything optimistic. The QUALITATIVE claim (posterior governed by the base rate, ~0.5 at prev=1−q, collapsing below) holds for any fixed sens, spec > chance, demonstrated on the pinned symmetric-0.99 constants and mechanism-explained (TP∝prev swamped by FP∝1−prev).
