## The folk belief
"Majority vote is self-correcting: if each agent is better than a coin flip, then adding more voters averages out their independent mistakes and the majority gets it right with probability approaching 1 (Condorcet). So more redundancy is more reliability ‚Äî build a bigger quorum, add more monitors/replicas/models to the vote, and the collective decision becomes as accurate as you like." The intuition treats each added voter as an INDEPENDENT draw whose error cancels the others' ‚Äî it prices in the Condorcet climb and never the shared error source that makes N agents behave like far fewer.

## The reliability thesis (reasoned to its fuller form ‚Äî Q-0254 duty)
The Condorcet Jury Theorem is exactly true ‚Äî **under independence**. If `N` (odd) jurors each vote correctly with probability `p>1/2` and their errors are INDEPENDENT, the probability the majority is correct strictly increases in `N` and tends to `1`: redundancy manufactures certainty. But fleets, quorums, and ensembles rarely fail independently. They share an error source ‚Äî one stale upstream, one training prior, one correlated feature, one common assumption ‚Äî so their mistakes arrive TOGETHER, and a majority cannot outvote an error the majority is making. Model the shared source with a canonical **one-factor latent-Gaussian probit**: agent `i` has a latent score

    X_i = ‚àöœÅ ¬∑ C + ‚àö(1‚àíœÅ) ¬∑ Œµ_i,   C, Œµ_i ~ N(0,1) iid,

and votes CORRECT iff `X_i > œÑ`, with `œÑ = Œ¶‚Åª¬π(1‚àíp)` so the **marginal** competence is `P(correct)=Œ¶(‚àíœÑ)=p` for every agent regardless of `œÅ`; the latent error-correlation between any two agents is `œÅ`. The single common factor `C` is the shared error source ‚Äî a good draw of `C` tilts everyone right, a bad draw tilts everyone wrong TOGETHER.

Condition on `C`: the votes are now independent with per-agent correct probability `q(C)=Œ¶((‚àöœÅ¬∑C ‚àí œÑ)/‚àö(1‚àíœÅ))`. By the law of large numbers the fraction correct ‚Üí `q(C)`, so as `N‚Üí‚àû` the majority is correct **iff `q(C)>1/2` iff `C > œÑ/‚àöœÅ`** ‚Äî the whole jury's verdict collapses onto which side of a threshold the common factor `C` landed. with `A_‚àû ‚Üí 1` only as `œÅ‚Üí0` (the Condorcet limit, `Œ¶‚Åª¬π(p)/‚àöœÅ‚Üí+‚àû`). This is the fact the folk belief misses: correlation does not merely SLOW the Condorcet climb, it **caps** it ‚Äî accuracy converges to a value strictly below certainty, set by `p` and `œÅ`, and no number of voters can push past it. Two consequences. **(a) The effective crowd is tiny.** Accuracy reaches within a hair of the ceiling by a modest `N‚âà1/œÅ` (here `1/œÅ‚âà3.3`); past that, extra voters are near-worthless ‚Äî a 501-agent correlated jury (0.6786) is barely better than a 15-agent one (0.6626) and much worse than a handful of INDEPENDENT agents. **(b) Correlation, not competence, is the wall.** The per-agent competence `p=0.60` is identical in the correlated and independent worlds; only `œÅ` differs, and only the œÅ>0 world plateaus below 1.
