# The tail-latency cure is almost free: a request served by one replica has a heavy-tailed latency whose 99th percentile is dominated by the occasional straggler, and the folk fix — duplicate every request and take the first answer — halves the tail but DOUBLES the load. Defer the second (hedged) request to the 95th-percentile delay instead, firing it only on the ~5% of requests whose primary is already running long, and you recover ~89% of full duplication's tail collapse at ≈5% extra load, not 100%. The last sliver of tail benefit that full duplication buys beyond the p95 hedge costs ~20× the load — the knee. Killing the tail is not "duplicate everything" — it is "hedge the slow few after p95."

## The phenomenon (one line)
A request served by one replica has a heavy-tailed service time; its 99th percentile is set by the occasional straggler. Send a **hedged** second copy only when the primary is still running at the 95th-percentile delay `d` — response `= min(primary, d + secondary)` — and on the pinned world the p99 latency drops from **≈322** to **≈61** while the hedge fires on only **≈5%** of requests. Full duplication (`min(primary, secondary)`, +100% load) drops p99 to **≈28** — a little lower, but it costs **20×** the extra load for that last stretch.

## The folk belief
"Tail latency is the price of scale, and the only real cure is redundancy: duplicate every request across two replicas and take whichever answers first. That halves the tail — but of course it doubles your load, so you ration it. And if you're going to hedge, hedge immediately; any delay before firing the second copy just leaves latency on the table." The intuition treats the tail cure as inherently expensive (a 2× load bill), treats the hedge delay as pure downside, and never separates "how much tail you remove" from "how much extra load you pay."

## The tail-cure thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The intuition conflates two independent quantities: the **tail reduction** a redundancy scheme buys and the **extra load** it costs. Full duplication maximizes the first (it always races two independent copies, so latency is `min(S1, S2)` and the tail only survives when BOTH copies are slow — probability `≈ p²` for a straggler fraction `p`) but also maximizes the second (a second request on EVERY call, +100% load). The key observation is that almost all of the tail reduction comes from rescuing the small minority of requests whose primary is a straggler — and you can identify those requests cheaply, in flight, by simply waiting: a primary that has not answered by the 95th-percentile delay `d` is, by construction, in the slow **5%**. So fire the hedge only then:

    latency = min(S1, d + S2)   when S1 > d,   else S1,   with d = the p95 of S.

The extra load is exactly `P(S > d) = 0.05` (the definition of the 95th percentile), a **twentieth** of full duplication's cost. Yet the tail collapses nearly as far, because the rescued straggler is retried on an **independent** replica whose fresh draw `S2` is fast **95%** of the time: a hedged straggler now completes around `d + (a fast time) ≈ 55–60`, and the only way to still be slow at the 99th percentile is for BOTH the primary AND the secondary to straggle — probability `≈ p² = 0.0025`, which sits past the 99.75th percentile, beyond p99. So `p99_hedge` is governed by the hedge offset `d ≈ 45`, not by the heavy tail.

Two consequences the folk belief misses. **(1) The tail cure is nearly free.** Measured as tail-reduction PER UNIT of extra load, the p95 hedge (`≈262` ms of p99 cut per `0.05` load `= ~5240` per unit) beats full duplication (`≈294` ms cut per `1.0` load `= ~294` per unit) by roughly **18×**. The expensive thing operators fear — killing the tail — is the cheap thing; it is *indiscriminate* duplication that is expensive. **(2) The last 10% of tail benefit is where all the cost hides — the knee.** Going from the p95 hedge to full duplication removes only the residual `p99_hedge − p99_dup ≈ 33` ms (it recovers the `≈11%` of full-dup's reduction the hedge left behind) but pays a **20×** load increase (`0.05 → 1.0`) to do it. The redundancy-vs-tail curve has a sharp knee at ~p95: nearly all the benefit sits below it at a few percent load, and the marginal load past it buys almost nothing. The honest reading: to kill the tail, hedge the slow few after ~p95 — do not duplicate everything, and do not hedge at zero delay (which is just full duplication under another name).

## The formal model (committed constants — sim-lab must reproduce exactly)
- Service time is a two-component **straggler mixture** (the canonical heavy tail): with probability `1 − P_STRAGGLER` a draw is `Exp(mean = MEAN_FAST)` (the common case), and with probability `P_STRAGGLER` it is `Exp(mean = MEAN_SLOW)` (a straggler). Every request/replica draws an INDEPENDENT service time via `rng.expovariate` — independent replicas are what make hedging work.
- Per replication, draw `N_REQUESTS` independent primary times `S1` and `N_REQUESTS` independent secondary times `S2`. The hedge delay `d` is the empirical 95th percentile of that batch's `S1` (nearest-rank).
- Three arms, per request: **baseline** latency `= S1` (load 1, no extra); **hedge@p95** latency `= min(S1, d + S2)` if `S1 > d` else `S1` (extra load `= [S1 > d]`, fires on ~5%); **full-dup** latency `= min(S1, S2)` (load 2, extra load `+1.0 = +100%`).
- Per replication record the batch **p99** of each arm and the **hedge-fire fraction**; also the per-trial **capture ratio** `(p99_base − p99_hedge)/(p99_base − p99_dup)` and the **efficiency edge** `red_hedge/load_hedge − red_dup/load_dup`.
- A statistic's mean and standard error are taken over `TRIALS = 200` independent replications, `se = sample-sd / √TRIALS` (the P104…P120 /se convention: z on an estimated statistic).
- Closed-form anchors are exact for the mixture: the CDF is `F(x) = (1−p)(1−e^{−x/mf}) + p(1−e^{−x/ms})`, inverted by bisection for the p95/p99 percentile anchors.

## Why it matters (cross-cutting)
Any fleet that fans a request across replicas and worries about tail latency — a scatter-gather query over N shards, a read served by any of R replicas, an agent step that calls a flaky downstream, a batch job whose completion waits on its slowest task — faces the same two mistakes: (1) treating the tail cure as inherently a 2× load bill (so rationing redundancy and living with the tail), and (2) hedging at zero delay when you do hedge (which IS full duplication, the expensive corner). Decision rule for an operator: hedge the slow few, not everything — set the hedge delay to ~the 95th percentile of the service-time distribution so the second copy fires only on the ~5% of requests already running long; budget ~5% extra load, not 100%; and stop there — the marginal tail benefit of pushing the hedge delay toward zero (toward full duplication) is small and costs up to 20× the load. Measure the redundancy scheme by tail-reduction-per-unit-load, not by tail-reduction alone, and the knee at ~p95 is where that ratio is maximized. (Real deployments refine this with *tied* requests — the two copies cross-cancel once one starts executing — which trims the residual duplicate work further; the p95-delay hedge already captures the first-order win.)

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The QUALITATIVE claim — a p95-delay hedge recovers most of full duplication's tail reduction at a few percent extra load, and the redundancy-vs-tail curve has a knee near p95 — holds for ANY heavy-tailed service-time distribution with independent replicas: the load of a p-percentile hedge is exactly `1 − p` by construction, and the hedged tail survives only when both copies straggle (`≈ (tail fraction)²`), so a fat tail makes the win larger, not smaller. The two-component exponential mixture is a pinned, reproducible instance of a heavy tail (a fast bulk plus a 5% straggler mass at 20× the mean); the exact percentile anchors (mixture p95 45.39, p99 321.89) rest on that specific mixture. The result depends on standard structural choices, all pinned: (a) replicas draw **independent** service times (correlated stragglers — a shared slow disk or hot node — weaken the hedge, since the secondary is then also likely slow; the independence is the load-bearing assumption and is stated, not hidden); (b) the hedge delay is the service-time p95 (a well-calibrated delay — a delay set too low degenerates toward full duplication, too high loses tail benefit); (c) the secondary is issued to a fresh independent replica and the first answer wins with no cancellation cost modeled (real *tied* requests cancel the loser to reclaim its work — a second-order refinement that only improves the load figure). The claim is scoped: for independent heavy-tailed replicas, hedging at the p95 delay recovers ~89% of full duplication's p99 collapse at ~5% extra load and beats it ~18× on tail-cut-per-unit-load, demonstrated on the pinned mixture, mechanism-explained (the `min` of independent draws + the `1−p` load identity), not asserted as a universal constant.
