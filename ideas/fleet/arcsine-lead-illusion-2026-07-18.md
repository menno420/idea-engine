## The phenomenon (one line)
Simulate a perfectly FAIR contest as an unbiased ±1 random walk (each step a fair coin, zero edge, zero drift) over N_STEPS steps, and measure the fraction of the game one side spends IN THE LEAD (ahead of even). That fraction does NOT concentrate near 1/2 — it follows the **arcsine distribution**, whose density `1/(π·√(x(1−x)))` is **U-shaped**: it piles up at 0 and 1 and is minimized at 1/2. So in a fair game one side tends to lead almost the entire time; on the pinned 500-step world one side leads for ≥90% or ≤10% of the play with probability **~0.4156**, while the balanced "each ahead 40–60% of the time" see-saw happens with probability only **~0.1305**.

## The folk belief
"If the game is perfectly fair — a 50/50 coin every step, no edge for either side — then the two players should trade the lead evenly. Each is ahead about half the time, the lead flips back and forth, and the fraction of time-in-the-lead clusters near 1/2. A player who leads almost the whole match must be better or luckier on average; a fair game produces a tight, see-saw contest." The intuition treats "fair in the odds" as "level in the lead," and expects the time-in-the-lead distribution to be bunched (unimodal) around 1/2.

## The probability thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The intuition confuses the **mean** of the time-in-the-lead with its **distribution**. A fair walk IS fair in expectation — the mean fraction of time in the lead is exactly 1/2 by the walk's sign symmetry. But the *distribution* of that fraction is the exact opposite of bunched: it is **bimodal at the extremes** (Lévy's first arcsine law).

The mechanism is that a fair random walk is **sticky about its sign**: once the partial sum `S_i` is on one side of zero, returning to zero is rare (the walk is null-recurrent — it returns with probability 1 but the expected time between returns is infinite), so long excursions on one side dominate. Formally, for a symmetric ±1 walk of `2n` steps, label segment `i` (from `S_{i-1}` to `S_i`) as ABOVE the axis iff `S_{i-1}+S_i > 0` — the two endpoints differ by 1, so the sum is odd and never 0, and the segment's side is always defined. The number `T` of above-axis segments is always even, and the **exact** discrete arcsine law (Feller, *An Introduction to Probability Theory*, Vol. 1, §III.4) is

    P(T = 2k) = u_{2k} · u_{2(n−k)},   u_{2j} = C(2j, j) / 4^j,   k = 0 … n,

a PRODUCT of two "still-at-zero" probabilities — maximal at the endpoints `k=0` and `k=n` and minimal in the middle. Its `n→∞` limit is the continuous first arcsine law: the fraction of time positive has CDF `P(fraction ≤ x) → (2/π)·arcsin(√x)`, whose density `1/(π√(x(1−x)))` is U-shaped. Two consequences the folk belief misses. **(1) The extremes dominate.** `P(fraction ≤ 0.1) = (2/π)arcsin(√0.1) = 0.204833` in the continuous limit — so a fair-game player is ~20% likely to be behind for MORE than 90% of the game, ~2× the naive ~10%; symmetrically one side leads for ≥90% or ≤10% of the play ~41% of the time. **(2) The middle is rarest.** `P(0.4 ≤ fraction ≤ 0.6) = (2/π)(arcsin√0.6 − arcsin√0.4) = 0.128132` — the "roughly even see-saw" the intuition expects to be typical is instead the LEAST likely region. The honest reading: a fair game is fair in the mean, not level in the lead; a long, one-sided lead is the SIGNATURE of a fair contest, not evidence of an edge. Reading "who has led most of the match" as "who is better" is a mistake whenever the underlying contest is close-to-fair.

## Why it matters (cross-cutting)
Any close, roughly-fair, back-and-forth process — a tied competitive match, an A/B test whose two arms are near-parity, two comparable trading strategies, two agents of similar skill, a cumulative-score race, a running vote margin — generates a "who has been ahead" signal that people read as evidence of an edge. The arcsine law says that even with ZERO edge, one side will typically be ahead for the vast majority of the contest, and a genuinely even see-saw is the least likely outcome — so a long, lopsided lead in a near-fair contest is the EXPECTED signature of fairness, not proof of superiority. Decision rule for an operator/analyst: do NOT infer an edge from "who led most of the time" or "how long the lead lasted" when the underlying contest is close-to-fair — that duration is arcsine-distributed and concentrates at the extremes by construction; measure the edge from the DRIFT (the net margin per step / the terminal gap and its standard error), not from lead duration; and expect long one-sided leads and rare see-saws as the normal texture of a fair game, budgeting confidence and stopping rules to the arcsine tails rather than to the naive "it should hover near 50/50" intuition.
