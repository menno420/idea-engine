## The phenomenon (one line)
Observe p quantities, each once, with unit-variance noise (`X_i ~ Normal(θ_i, 1)`). The obvious estimator is the sample value itself — `θ̂_i = X_i` (maximum-likelihood, unbiased). For **p ≥ 3** it is **beatable for every θ**: the James-Stein estimator `θ̂_JS = (1 − (p−2)/‖X‖²)·X` (shrinking every coordinate toward the origin) has strictly smaller **total** expected squared error. On the pinned world (p=10 unrelated true means, unit noise) the simulated MLE total risk is **10.009055** while the James-Stein total risk is **6.052289** — a ~40% cut in total squared error — advantage **3.956766** against the exact closed form **3.952936**; and at θ=0 the James-Stein risk collapses to **2** for every dimension. At the **p=2** boundary the shrinkage factor is identically 1, so James-Stein equals the MLE and the advantage is exactly **0** — the dominance switches on precisely at p≥3.

## The folk belief
"To estimate a quantity, use its own measurement. The sample value `X_i` is unbiased and maximum-likelihood, so it is the best estimator of `θ_i` — you cannot do better with the data you have. And borrowing strength across UNRELATED problems is meaningless: a batting average tells you nothing about the price of tea in China or the mass of a candy bar, so estimating them jointly cannot beat estimating each separately. Shrinking an estimate toward some arbitrary point just injects bias and makes it worse." The intuition treats each coordinate as its own sealed problem and reads any pooling of unrelated estimands as a category error.

## The decision-theoretic thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The intuition confuses PER-COORDINATE optimality with TOTAL-RISK optimality, and confuses "unbiased" with "admissible." The loss being minimized is the **summed** squared error `R(θ̂, θ) = E[Σ_i (θ̂_i − θ_i)²]`. The MLE `θ̂=X` has constant risk `R_MLE = p` for every θ (each coordinate contributes its unit noise variance). Stein (1956) proved that for **p ≥ 3** this estimator is **inadmissible**: James & Stein (1961) exhibited a strictly better one. Shrinking `X` toward a fixed point (the origin) by the data-dependent factor `1 − (p−2)/‖X‖²`,

    θ̂_JS = (1 − (p−2)/S)·X,   S = ‖X‖²,

trades a small bias for a large variance reduction. Because squared error is convex and the p coordinates are SUMMED, the aggregate wins even though for a single coordinate the shrinkage can hurt. The exact risk (shrink toward origin, `λ = ‖θ‖²`) is

    R_JS(θ) = p − (p−2)²·E[1/S],   S ~ noncentral χ²_p(λ),

with `E[1/S] = Σ_k Pois(k; λ/2)/(p + 2k − 2)` finite and positive **iff p ≥ 3**. Hence `R_JS(θ) < p = R_MLE(θ)` for **every** θ — uniform dominance. At `θ = 0` (`λ = 0`) it collapses to `R_JS = p − (p−2) = 2` for EVERY dimension: ten unit-noise coordinates estimated at total squared error **2** instead of **10**.

Two facts the "estimate each separately" instinct misses. **(1) The coordinates need not be related.** The improvement is a geometric fact about p-dimensional noise — `‖X‖²` concentrates around `p + λ`, so `(p−2)/‖X‖²` is a usable estimate of the optimal shrinkage — not a fact about the problems sharing structure. Pooling a batting average with a candy-bar weight is legitimate when TOTAL MSE is the objective; the "unrelated" part is exactly what makes it a paradox. **(2) The p ≥ 3 threshold is exact.** For `p = 1` the MLE is admissible (nothing dominates it uniformly); for `p = 2` the coefficient `(p−2) = 0` makes the shrinkage vanish and James-Stein equals the MLE identically. The dominance is not "shrinkage always helps" — it is a sharp dimensional phenomenon that appears precisely at three simultaneous estimates. (The gain grows with dimension: the advantage scales like `(p−2)²·E[1/‖X‖²]`.)

## Why it matters (cross-cutting)
Any time an operator/analyst estimates SEVERAL noisy quantities at once and cares about total accuracy — per-cohort conversion rates, per-agent latencies, per-item demand forecasts, per-region churn, A/B lifts across many arms, per-model eval scores — the separately-optimal answer (each quantity's own mean) is jointly beatable at p≥3. Decision rule: (1) count how many quantities you are estimating simultaneously from noisy single reads — at ≥3, the sample-mean/MLE is inadmissible under total squared error, and a shrinkage estimator (James-Stein toward a common target, or empirical-Bayes toward the grand mean) strictly lowers total MSE, the gain scaling like `(p−2)²·E[1/‖X‖²]` with the number pooled; (2) the win does NOT require the quantities to be related — the improvement is a high-dimensional-noise geometry fact, so pooling unrelated estimands is legitimate when total MSE is the objective; (3) the shrink TARGET is a modeling choice (origin, grand mean, a regression fit) that trades a little per-coordinate bias for a large aggregate variance cut — put it where the truths cluster, and the extreme individual estimates (the ones most likely to be noise) are pulled in the most. This is the statistical backbone of every "regularize / partial-pool / borrow strength" method (ridge, empirical-Bayes, hierarchical/random-effects models): they are all Stein shrinkage, and they are provably better, not just conservative.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The exact anchor `R_JS(θ) = p − (p−2)²·E[1/‖X‖²]` is the risk of the James-Stein estimator shrinking toward a fixed point under Gaussian observation noise `X~Normal(θ, σ²I)` with KNOWN σ (here σ=1) and summed squared-error loss; it is a theorem (Stein 1956; James & Stein 1961), and the p≥3 inadmissibility of the MLE is exact. The claim rests on standard structural choices, all pinned: (a) unit-variance Gaussian noise `X_i~Normal(θ_i,1)` — the classical setup; unequal known variances rescale to it, and the qualitative dominance survives, but the exact `(p−2)` coefficient is for the equal-variance case; (b) shrink toward the ORIGIN with the plain (non-positive-part) James-Stein factor — the positive-part variant `(1−(p−2)/S)_+` dominates it further and shrinking toward the grand mean (Efron-Morris) costs one degree of freedom `(p−3)`, but the plain origin form is the cleanest exact anchor; (c) the true means θ are a fixed "unrelated" spread drawn once from `Normal(0, TAU²)` — the dominance holds for ANY fixed θ (that is the theorem), and TAU only sets `λ=‖θ‖²` and hence the SIZE of the gain (larger ‖θ‖ shrinks the advantage toward 0 but never to it), so the pinned TAU=1 just makes the gain clearly ≥3σ-detectable. The QUALITATIVE claim — the MLE is inadmissible for p≥3 and James-Stein shrinkage strictly lowers total squared error for every θ, with the gain vanishing exactly at p=2 — is the classical Stein result, so the pinned constants just make the anchor exact and the seed reproducible. The claim is scoped: for unit-variance Gaussian simultaneous estimation under summed squared-error loss the sample mean is inadmissible at p≥3 and James-Stein shrinkage toward the origin dominates it, demonstrated on the pinned constants, mechanism-explained (‖X‖² concentration) with an exact p=2 boundary control, not asserted as a claim about biased estimation in general or about non-quadratic loss.
