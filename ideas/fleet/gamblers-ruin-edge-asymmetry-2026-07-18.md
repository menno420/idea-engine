## The phenomenon (one line)
A gambler starts with `i` units and bets one FIXED unit per round against a house; each round the fortune moves +1 with probability p and −1 with probability q=1−p, and play stops the first time the fortune hits 0 (ruin) or a target N (cash out). The probability of reaching the target N before ruin — the two-barrier first-passage probability — is **not** a gentle linear function of the edge; it is `P = (1−(q/p)^i)/(1−(q/p)^N)` for p≠0.5 and `i/N` for a fair game, and because it rides the barrier ratio `(q/p)^N` it collapses EXPONENTIALLY in the target distance N once p drops below ½. On the pinned world: CFG_FAIR (p=0.5, i=20, N=40) gives P=0.5 exactly; CFG_MOD (p=0.48, i=20, N=40) gives P=0.16786; CFG_EDGE (p=0.485, i=100, N=200) gives P=0.0024682 — a fair symmetric start (i=N/2) worth 0.5 collapses to ~1-in-405 once a 1.5% edge is applied over a target distance of N=200.

## The folk belief
"The house edge is tiny — a 1.5% edge only shaves a percent or two off my chances. If a fair game gives me a 50% shot at doubling up, a game where I win 48.5% of rounds instead of 50% gives me roughly 48.5% — the penalty scales LINEARLY with the edge, so a small edge is a small cost." The intuition maps the per-round disadvantage straight onto the whole-session outcome (a 1.5% worse coin → a ~1.5% worse chance of cashing out), and forgets that the session is a compounding boundary-crossing problem: to travel from `i` up to N against a per-step drift, every one of the many steps pays the edge, and the disadvantages MULTIPLY through the barrier ratio (q/p) raised to the target distance. Accuracy-of-one-round is confused with probability-of-the-whole-session.

## The probabilistic thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
Let `P_i` be the probability of reaching N before 0 starting from fortune `i`. First-step analysis (condition on the first bet) gives the linear recurrence `P_i = p·P_{i+1} + q·P_{i−1}` with boundary conditions `P_0 = 0`, `P_N = 1`. Its characteristic equation `p·x² − x + q = 0` has roots `x = 1` and `x = q/p`, so for p≠q the general solution is `P_i = A + B(q/p)^i`; fitting the boundaries gives

    P_i = (1 − (q/p)^i) / (1 − (q/p)^N)     (p ≠ ½),

and for the fair game p=q=½ the double root collapses the solution to the linear `P_i = i/N`. Write `r = q/p` (the odds ratio against you). Two facts the folk belief misses. **(1) The dependence on N is EXPONENTIAL, not linear.** For p<½ we have r>1, so `r^N` grows exponentially in the target distance; from a symmetric start `i = N/2`, `P = (1−r^{N/2})/(1−r^N) = 1/(1+r^{N/2}) ≈ r^{−N/2}` for large N — the reach probability decays like `(p/q)^{N/2}`, geometric in N. Doubling the distance you must travel squares the penalty. **(2) The penalty is exponential in the edge THROUGH N, not proportional to it.** A per-round edge of ε (so p=½−ε/2 in a symmetric parameterization, `r = (½+ε/2)/(½−ε/2) ≈ 1+2ε` for small ε) enters the exponent: from `i=N/2`, `P ≈ 1/(1 + (1+2ε)^{N/2}) ≈ 1/(1 + e^{εN})` for small ε — so the reach probability is governed by the product `εN`, and a fixed small edge ε produces an arbitrarily large collapse as N grows. On the pinned CFG_EDGE (ε≈0.03 per the symmetric read, N=200) the product `εN ≈ 6`, `e^{6} ≈ 403`, and indeed P ≈ 1/405 = 0.002466 — a ~200× collapse from the fair-game 0.5, NOT the ~1.5% a linear-in-ε intuition predicts. The honest reading: against a house edge, the probability of hitting a distant target is exponentially small in the distance, so "grind a small edge to a big target" is a losing proposition whose failure probability the linear intuition wildly under-states; the only levers that matter are shrinking the target distance N (cash out early), erasing the drift (get p to ½), or accepting that with p<½ and large N ruin is all but certain.

## Why it matters (cross-cutting)
Any decision that grinds a small per-round disadvantage toward a distant target inherits the exponential ruin asymmetry. Decision rule for an operator/designer/risk owner: (1) against a house edge the probability of hitting a distant target is EXPONENTIALLY small in the target distance N — `P ≈ 1/(1+e^{εN})` from a symmetric start with per-round edge ε — so a "small" edge is not a small penalty when N is large; the reach probability collapses geometrically, not linearly; (2) the only levers that move it are shrinking the target distance (cash out at a nearer N), erasing the drift (get p to ½), or increasing the stake so fewer bets are needed (bold play — which trades a higher variance for a shorter walk); (3) this governs bankroll survival in fixed-stake betting, martingale/double-down systems (which shorten the walk but do NOT beat the edge — the barrier ratio is invariant to the doubling), reliability/inventory buffers drained by a slightly-adverse net flow (a buffer of size N against a small negative drift fails almost surely, exponentially in N), and any "grind a tiny statistical edge to a big goal against a slightly-adverse process" plan. Report the reach/survival probability at the DEPLOYED target distance and drift, never the per-round edge, because the whole-session outcome is exponential in the distance the linear intuition treats as free.
