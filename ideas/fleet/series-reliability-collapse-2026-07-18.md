## The phenomenon (one line)
A SERIES system works only while ALL of its N components work, so it fails at the FIRST component failure — its lifetime is the MINIMUM of the N per-component lifetimes. For independent exponential lifetimes (rate λ, per-component MTBF T=1/λ), the minimum of N of them is itself exponential with rate Nλ (the hazard rates ADD: λ_sys=∑λ_i=Nλ), so the system MTBF is exactly T/N. Twenty identical parts each with MTBF T=1 give a system with MTBF 0.05 — twenty times MORE failure-prone than a single part — and reliability R_sys(t)=r(t)^N decays geometrically in N. The symmetric fully-parallel system (fails only at the LAST component failure, lifetime = MAXIMUM of N) has MTBF T·H_N≈3.60 at N=20: twenty-fold redundancy multiplies MTBF by only ~3.6×.

## The folk belief
"Every component is 99% reliable — practically perfect — so a system built from good components is a good system." Reliability intuition is additive and anchored on the parts: a chain of excellent links feels excellent, and adding one more excellent component feels neutral or helpful. The folk error is reading system reliability off the components' individual quality instead of off the series product, and expecting a linear (∼N·(1−r)) degradation when the real one is geometric (r^N) and the MTBF penalty is a full division by N — so the "20% headroom" of twenty 99%-reliable parts is actually a system that is 82% reliable (0.99^20), and 69 such parts are a coin flip (0.99^69≈0.50).

## The thesis (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
A series system is a logical AND over its components: it is up iff every component is up. Under independence its reliability at any time is therefore the PRODUCT R_sys(t)=∏ r_i(t) — never a sum, never an average — so it is bounded above by the WEAKEST component and falls monotonically as components are added (each factor r_i<1 shrinks the product). That is the reliability-at-a-time statement; the lifetime statement is sharper. The system's time-to-failure is the time of the first component failure: T_sys = min_i T_i. For independent exponential lifetimes T_i∼Exp(λ_i), the minimum of exponentials is exponential with the SUMMED rate — P(min>t)=∏P(T_i>t)=∏e^{−λ_i t}=e^{−(∑λ_i)t} — so T_sys∼Exp(∑λ_i) and the hazard rates ADD. For N identical parts (λ_i=λ, T=1/λ) that is λ_sys=Nλ and

  MTBF_sys = 1/λ_sys = 1/(Nλ) = T/N.

The counterintuitive core: N components each with the SAME excellent MTBF T produce a system whose MTBF is not T, not a modest haircut below T, but T/N — the system is N times more failure-prone than any one of its parts, and nothing about the parts' individual excellence enters except through the count N. The collapse is not a bad part (each part in isolation keeps the full MTBF T) and not sampling noise (it does not shrink with the number of simulated systems; the anchor is exact); it is manufactured entirely by the AND/minimum structure of seriesing.

Now the symmetric counterpart — REDUNDANCY. A fully parallel system is up while ANY component is up (a logical OR), so it fails at the LAST failure: T_par = max_i T_i. For N identical exponentials the expected maximum is E[max]=T·H_N where H_N=∑_{k=1}^{N}1/k is the N-th harmonic number — GROWING only LOGARITHMICALLY in N. So the two structures are radically asymmetric: seriesing N parts DIVIDES MTBF by N (linear collapse), while paralleling N parts MULTIPLIES it by only H_N≈ln N+γ (logarithmic gain). At N=20: series MTBF T/N=0.05, parallel MTBF T·H_20≈3.598 — a chain of twenty is 20× worse, a redundant bank of twenty is only ~3.6× better. The design lever the mechanism exposes: reliability is bought by REMOVING series elements and by REDUNDANCY (parallel), and is destroyed almost for free by adding series elements — the exact opposite of the "each part is good, so more good parts is fine" intuition.

## Why it matters (transferable mechanism)
Any system that is up only while ALL of its independent parts are up — a request path through N microservices, a pipeline of N sequential stages, a supply chain of N single-sourced links, a workflow of N manual approvals, a spacecraft with N non-redundant subsystems — has reliability equal to the PRODUCT of its parts' reliabilities and, for exponential lifetimes, an MTBF of T/N, not T. The transferable audit: whenever reliability (or availability, or yield) is quoted per-component and the components sit in series, multiply — do not average or anchor on the weakest — and expect the MTBF to DIVIDE by the series count; the fix is to REMOVE series elements (shorten the chain), add REDUNDANCY on the elements that dominate the failure-rate sum (parallel buys T·H_N, so redundancy pays only where a single element carries a large share of ∑λ_i), and treat "each part is 99% reliable" as a statement about ONE factor of a product that has many factors. As a fleet-external pure-mechanism head there is NO lane build here; the deliverable is a citable measured verdict plus the "series reliability is the product and series MTBF is T/N — add redundancy or remove series elements, never read system reliability off the parts' individual quality" correction. Verdict co-consumer = any fleet or product surface sizing end-to-end availability from per-component numbers (an N-hop request path's uptime SLO, a multi-stage pipeline's success rate, a checklist/approval chain's completion odds), or deciding where to spend a reliability budget (harden the chain-length vs harden a part vs add redundancy).

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The exact anchors T/N (series) and T·H_N (parallel) depend on structural assumptions: (a) INDEPENDENT component lifetimes (no common-cause failure) — the reliability product ∏r_i and the rate sum ∑λ_i both assume independence; a shared shock (correlated failure) makes the series product LESS pessimistic than r^N and is a different regime (the correlated-failure head, not this one); (b) EXPONENTIAL lifetimes (constant hazard, memoryless) — under a general lifetime the series reliability is still the product ∏r_i(t) and the system still fails at the minimum, but the MTBF is no longer exactly T/N (it is ∫∏R_i(t)dt, which equals T/N only for the exponential); (c) a pure SERIES (logical AND) topology for the collapse arm and a pure PARALLEL (logical OR) topology for the descriptive arm. But the qualitative result — a series system's reliability is the PRODUCT and degrades monotonically as components are added, so more "good" parts in series is a WORSE system, while redundancy helps only with strongly diminishing (∼logarithmic, for the exponential) returns — is robust for any lifetime distribution and any positive component count. The claim is scoped: under independent Exp(λ) component lifetimes with N in series, the system MTBF is exactly T/N and R_sys(t)=r(t)^N, demonstrated on the pinned constants, mechanism-explained (min-of-exponentials / hazard addition), not asserted as a universal numeric law for non-exponential or correlated lifetimes.
