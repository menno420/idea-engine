## The phenomenon (one line)
A bullet fund's IRR is moic^(1/T) − 1, so for a FIXED amount of money returned a shorter horizon strictly raises the IRR: a fund that returns a small multiple quickly out-IRRs a fund that returns a larger multiple slowly, and ranking funds on IRR therefore selects, systematically, the one that hands LPs less cash — while a subscription credit line that delays the LP capital call shortens the IRR clock and raises the reported IRR even as LPs, net of line interest, receive fewer dollars.

## The folk belief
"IRR is the headline number: the fund with the higher IRR is the better fund, and a higher IRR means LPs made more money." LPs, league tables, and fund marketing rank managers by realised IRR as if it were a stand-in for dollars returned — treating a time-normalised rate as though it were the money multiple.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
IRR is the root r of NPV(r) = 0; for the minimal bullet fund (invest 1 at t=0, receive the money-multiple M at t=T) that root is exactly r = M^(1/T) − 1. Two funds can be ordered oppositely by M and by r: holding M fixed, r falls as T grows (a later payout is a lower rate); holding T fixed, r rises with M. So a fund with a LARGER M but a LONGER T can post a LOWER IRR than a fund with a SMALLER M over a SHORTER T. Concretely, 2.4x in 10 years is r = 2.4^(1/10) − 1 = 9.15%, while 1.3x in 18 months is r = 1.3^(1/1.5) − 1 = 19.11%: the second fund returns LPs barely more than half the cash yet posts more than double the IRR. Ranking on IRR picks it.

The load-bearing companion is the subscription line. A capital-call line lets the fund draw on a bank facility to fund the investment at t=0 and defer the LP capital call to t=τ, paying the LP capital in at τ and settling the line's interest out of the eventual distribution. Model the LP's own cash flows: without the line the LP pays 1 at t=0 and receives M at t=T, so IRR = M^(1/T) − 1 and MOIC = M. With the line the LP pays 1 at t=τ and receives M − ((1+c)^τ − 1) at t=T, where c is the line rate and (1+c)^τ − 1 the interest borne — so the LP's MOIC strictly FALLS (they got fewer dollars), while the IRR, now solving (1+r)^(T−τ) = M − ((1+c)^τ − 1), runs on the shorter clock T − τ and RISES. Same underlying deal, fewer dollars to the LP, a higher reported IRR: the metric moved on timing alone, not on value created. The rational reading is that IRR conflates the SPEED of cash flows with their MAGNITUDE; the dollars-to-LPs question is answered by MOIC / DPI, not by IRR.

## Caveats & crossovers (honest disclosure)
- **The claim is about IRR-vs-dollars, not about IRR being "wrong."** IRR is a correct time-normalised rate; the head is that it is NOT a proxy for dollars returned, so ranking or marketing on IRR mis-ranks funds by cash to LPs. DPI / MOIC / TVPI answer the dollars question.
- **Bullet idealisation for G1/G2, relaxed in G3.** G1 and G2 use single-distribution bullets to isolate the mechanism cleanly; G3 shows the sign survives staged calls and spread distributions, so the effect is not a bullet artifact. It is not a claim about every possible cash-flow shape — the inversion fraction is ~99.5% (G1) / ~88.6% (G3), not 100%, and both are reported.
- **Subscription-line interest is netted honestly.** G2 charges the line's compounded interest against the LP distribution, so the higher IRR comes alongside a strictly LOWER MOIC — the sharp, honest form. A zero-cost idealisation would only widen the IRR gain; real lines cost interest, which the model includes.
- **Crossover with the annual-prepay implied-APR trap (P130) and NPV-vs-IRR.** All three are time-value-of-money results; the annual-prepay head is a borrowing-cost claim and the classic NPV-vs-IRR conflict is a project-ranking claim. This head is scoped to the venture-fund performance metric: rank-inversion on IRR versus dollars-to-LPs, plus the subscription-line inflation. No overlap with the cap-table heads (option-pool-shuffle, founder-dilution-waterfall, full-ratchet), which hold horizon fixed and move ownership; here ownership is irrelevant and the horizon is the lever.
- **Magnitudes are illustrative; direction is scale-robust.** The exact IRR gaps depend on the pinned M/T bands, but the sign (shorter horizon at fixed money → higher IRR) is an algebraic property of M^(1/T)−1 for every M>1 and T>0, which the gates test.
