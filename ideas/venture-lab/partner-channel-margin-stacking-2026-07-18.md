The partner's margin is not a slice handed off a fixed pie; the second independent markup SHRINKS the pie.

## The phenomenon (one line)
A manufacturer marks up over its cost C to a wholesale price w; an independent reseller treats w as ITS cost and marks up again to a retail price P. Each optimizes its own margin taking the other's as given, so the retail price P_dec exceeds the price P_int a single integrated seller (marking up ONCE, from C) would set — and the two firms' combined profit is strictly below the integrated firm's. The extra markup is deadweight: it raises price, halves quantity, and leaves the whole channel poorer, not just re-slices a fixed pie.

## The folk belief
"A channel partner takes a cut — we keep list price minus their margin — so adding a distributor/reseller/marketplace is subtractive: we trade a known margin slice for reach, and the pie is otherwise unchanged." Operators price the partner decision as `net = list − partner_margin`, treat the partner's margin as a transfer, and under-book the damage — missing that the partner sets ITS price independently, that the second markup lifts the STREET price above the direct-sale monopoly price, cuts unit volume, and shrinks the combined (vendor + partner) profit below going direct. They also assume "more layers = just more cuts to sum," when each independent markup compounds the loss multiplicatively.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
Take linear demand: a consumer buys one unit iff their willingness-to-pay v exceeds the price. With v ~ Uniform[0, VMAX], demand at price P is Q(P) = 1 − P/VMAX (as a fraction of the market), i.e. linear with intercept 1 and slope 1/VMAX. Unit cost is C.

**Integrated seller (one markup).** Maximize (P − C)·Q(P) = (P − C)(1 − P/VMAX). FOC → P_int = (VMAX + C)/2, quantity Q_int = (VMAX − C)/(2·VMAX), profit π_int = (VMAX − C)²/(4·VMAX).

**Decentralized channel (two markups, in series).**
- *Reseller*, facing wholesale w as its cost: max (P − w)(1 − P/VMAX) → best response P_ret(w) = (VMAX + w)/2, buying Q(P_ret(w)) = (VMAX − w)/(2·VMAX) units.
- *Manufacturer*, anticipating the reseller: max (w − C)·Q(P_ret(w)) = (w − C)(VMAX − w)/(2·VMAX) → w* = (VMAX + C)/2.
- Substituting: retail P_dec = P_ret(w*) = (3·VMAX + C)/4, quantity Q_dec = (VMAX − C)/(4·VMAX).
- Profits: manufacturer (w* − C)·Q_dec = (VMAX − C)²/(8·VMAX); reseller (P_dec − w*)·Q_dec = (VMAX − C)²/(16·VMAX); **combined** π_channel = 3·(VMAX − C)²/(16·VMAX).

Consequences the "the partner just takes a cut" instinct misses:

1. **The independent channel RAISES the street price.** P_dec − P_int = (3·VMAX + C)/4 − (VMAX + C)/2 = (VMAX − C)/4 > 0. Two hands on the pricing lever, each marking up over the other's margin, push price PAST the monopoly optimum — not below it, above it.

2. **The pie SHRINKS — deadweight loss, not redistribution.** π_channel / π_int = [3(VMAX−C)²/16VMAX] / [(VMAX−C)²/4VMAX] = 3/4, at quantity Q_dec / Q_int = 1/2. The manufacturer AND reseller COMBINED earn 3/4 of what one integrated firm earns, selling half the units. The higher price destroys mutually-beneficial trades the integrated firm would have kept; consumers pay more and buy less. Nobody in the channel is better off than integration — the second markup is pure loss split three ways (vendor, partner, and consumer surplus).

3. **Layers compound.** Each additional independent margin-setting stage (distributor → VAR → retailer) adds another markup over the prior stage's margin, pushing price up and the combined pie down again — the loss is multiplicative in the number of independent layers, not additive in their cuts.

On the pinned world (VMAX≈100, C=20): P_int=60, P_dec=80 (retail +33%), Q_dec=½·Q_int, π_channel=¾·π_int. The 20-point price bump and the quartered profit are the double markup, not the partner's "fair cut."

## Why it matters (venture-ops)
Channel strategy — distributors, resellers, VARs, marketplaces, agencies — is a first-order go-to-market decision, and the folk model prices it as "list minus the partner's cut." This proposal gives the operator a hard channel-economics identity: two independent firms marking up in series push the street price ABOVE the direct-sale monopoly price and shrink the combined pie below the integrated profit — the partner's margin is not a transfer off a fixed pie, it is a second markup that destroys trades. Concretely here: adding one independent reseller lifts retail 60→80 (+33%), halves unit volume, and leaves vendor+partner with 3/4 of the direct profit. Decision rules: (1) evaluate a channel partner on the combined-pie effect (price up, quantity down, total margin shrunk), not "list minus their cut"; (2) if you must use an independent channel, kill the second independent markup with a contract — impose a resale price / price ceiling, a two-part tariff (wholesale AT COST plus a fixed franchise fee), or a revenue-share that makes the partner price as if it owned the full margin (the standard double-marginalization cures); (3) treat every additional independent margin-setting layer (distributor → VAR → retailer) as a multiplicative loss, not an additive cut — collapse layers or align their incentives; (4) vertical integration (or agency/consignment where YOU set the price) is the clean cure when the channel margin is large. The trap is booking a channel partner as a fixed-margin transfer and missing that its independent markup raises the price, shrinks the volume, and leaves the whole channel — including you — poorer than going direct.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The core inequalities are model-robust for ANY downward-sloping demand where both stages have market power: a second independent firm adding a positive markup over the first firm's margin raises the final price above the single-markup (integrated) price, reduces quantity, and — because the integrated price already maximizes total channel profit — lowers the COMBINED profit (the integrated outcome is the joint-profit maximum by construction, so any decentralized deviation weakly lowers total profit; with a strictly positive second markup the loss is strict). This direction (price up, combined pie down) is the general double-marginalization result and does not depend on the demand shape. What IS model-dependent is the SIZE — hence the exact 3/4 profit ratio and 1/2 quantity ratio: those clean fractions are specific to LINEAR demand (constant slope), the pinned world here (Uniform[0,VMAX] willingness-to-pay ⇒ linear demand). A different demand curve (constant-elasticity, logit) changes the exact ratios but not the qualitative facts (P_dec>P_int, π_channel<π_int, Q_dec<Q_int) whenever both stages independently mark up. Assumptions: (a) both stages have and exercise pricing power (a perfectly-competitive reseller sets price=cost and there is no second markup — the effect requires the partner to earn a margin, which is exactly when the trap is quoted); (b) linear (Uniform-WTP) demand for the exact 3/4 and 1/2 anchors — the direction holds generally, the fractions are the linear-demand instance; (c) a single manufacturer → single reseller two-stage channel, wholesale-price (not two-part-tariff) contract (the two-part tariff is precisely the CURE, which is the point — a franchise-fee/RPM/revenue-share contract restores the integrated outcome); (d) one period, deterministic marginal cost, no fixed costs. The claim is scoped: on linear demand with an independent margin-setting reseller under a simple wholesale-price contract — the standard channel description — the successive markups push the retail price above the integrated monopoly price and cut the combined channel profit to 3/4 at half the quantity, demonstrated on the pinned constants and mechanism-explained via the two-stage FOCs (Cournot/Spengler double marginalization), not asserted as a universal law. The price-up / pie-down direction holds for any two independent margin-setting stages on downward-sloping demand; the pinned VMAX/C and the 3/4·1/2 fractions are the linear-demand illustration and bracket the effect.
