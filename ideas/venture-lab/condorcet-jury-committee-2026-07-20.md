## The phenomenon (one line)
A committee of n=101 independent members who are each individually right 60% of the time reaches the correct majority verdict ≈97.9% of the time (climbing toward 1 as it grows), but the SAME 101-member committee whose members are each right only 40% of the time is correct just ≈2.1% of the time — worse than a single 40% member (0.40), and getting monotonically worse with every member added; only a committee of exactly-coin-flip (p=1/2) members stays pinned at 50% for every size.

## The folk belief
"More heads are better than one — put it to a committee." The intuition treats a group vote as automatically more reliable than any individual: add reviewers, enlarge the panel, widen the quorum, and the majority washes out individual mistakes toward the right answer. It prices in only the Condorcet climb (p>1/2 ⇒ bigger is better) and never asks which side of the coin-flip line the members sit on — so it recommends enlarging a panel of mediocre-or-worse members exactly when enlarging makes the collective decision reliably WRONG.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
Let n (odd, so no ties) independent members each vote for the correct alternative with probability p and against it with 1−p. The number correct is Binomial(n,p), and the majority is correct iff more than n/2 vote correctly, so

    M(n,p) = Σ_{k=(n+1)/2}^{n} C(n,k) · p^k · (1−p)^{n−k}.

The load-bearing structural fact is the sign of (p − 1/2). By the weak law of large numbers the fraction voting correctly concentrates on p; a strict majority is correct in the limit iff p > 1/2. So as n → ∞, M(n,p) → 1 for p > 1/2, → 0 for p < 1/2, and sits at exactly 1/2 for p = 1/2 (every odd n). The finite-n statement is the same in miniature: for p > 1/2, M is strictly increasing in n (each added pair of members strictly raises the probability the majority is right); for p < 1/2, M is strictly DECREASING in n — each added pair strictly lowers it, driving the committee toward certain error. The maximizer of M over jury size is therefore n = ∞ when p > 1/2 and n = 1 (a single member) when p < 1/2. Committee SIZE is an amplifier: it magnifies whichever side of 1/2 the individual competence sits on, and it cannot manufacture reliability out of members who are individually worse than a coin.

Two consequences for governance. **(a) Vet competence before size.** Enlarging an investment committee, hiring panel, or review board is a bet that each marginal member clears p = 1/2 on the question at hand; below that line, every seat you add makes the panel's verdict reliably worse, and the honest move is to SHRINK it (in the p<1/2 limit, defer to one member). **(b) The threshold is the whole ballgame.** The competence gap between p = 0.55 and p = 0.45 is small per member, but at n = 101 it is the difference between a ~90%-right committee and a ~10%-right one — the majority mechanism converts a hair either side of the coin-flip line into near-certainty of the corresponding sign.
