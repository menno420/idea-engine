## The phenomenon (one line)
Demand D ~ Binomial(40, 1/2) has mean = median = 20, yet for a high-margin product (sell price p=10, unit cost c=2, salvage s=0 → critical ratio (p−c)/(p−s) = 4/5) the profit-maximizing order quantity is Q\* = 23, three units ABOVE the mean — because Q\* is the smallest Q with CDF(Q) ≥ 4/5, a quantile of demand, and the same closed form drops a low-margin good (c=8 → CR=1/5) to Q\* = 17 below the mean and pins a balanced good (c=5 → CR=1/2) at exactly the median 20.

## The folk belief
"Order what you expect to sell." The intuitive stocking rule reads mean (expected) demand as the target order quantity — buy 20 when you expect to sell 20 — treating over- and under-stocking as symmetric mistakes that a central estimate splits evenly. It has no place for the margin: it stocks a fat-margin luxury good and a thin-margin commodity to the same expected-demand number.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The order quantity is not an estimate of demand; it is a decision under an asymmetric payoff, and the asymmetry is set by the margin. Stocking one extra unit that does NOT sell costs the overage c − s (you paid c, recover salvage s); NOT stocking a unit that WOULD have sold costs the underage p − c (the forgone margin). The (Q+1)-th unit is worth, in expectation, its probability of selling times the underage minus its probability of sitting times the overage: ΔE(Q) = (p−c)·P(D ≥ Q+1) − (c−s)·P(D ≤ Q). With salvage s this rearranges exactly to ΔE(Q) = (p−s)·(CR − F(Q)) where CR = (p−c)/(p−s) is the critical ratio and F is the demand CDF. The marginal unit is worth adding precisely while F(Q) < CR, so you keep stocking until the CDF first reaches the critical ratio: Q\* = smallest Q with F(Q) ≥ CR. That is a QUANTILE of demand, not its mean.

The load-bearing structural fact is where the critical ratio sits relative to 1/2. A high margin makes the underage (p−c) large relative to the overage (c−s), pushes CR above 1/2, and drives Q\* into the upper tail — strictly ABOVE the median, because you would rather eat several unsold units than miss one fat-margin sale. A thin margin does the reverse: CR below 1/2, Q\* below the median. Only at CR = 1/2 (underage = overage) does the optimum land on the median, which for a symmetric demand equals the mean — the single knife-edge where the folk "order the mean" rule is correct. The folk rule silently assumes that knife-edge for every product; the critical fractile prices the margin in, and for anything more profitable than break-even-symmetric it says: deliberately overstock.
