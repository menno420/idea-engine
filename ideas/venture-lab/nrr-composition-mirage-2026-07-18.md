## The phenomenon (one line)
NRR = Σr_i·m_i/Σr_i is dollar-weighted, so it weights each account by its revenue share w_i=r_i/Σr. When large accounts (high w_i) also have the high retention multiple m_i (they survive and expand) and small accounts (low w_i) churn, the weighted mean is pulled toward the whales and reads NRR>100% — "negative net churn" — while the count-weighted logo retention (mean survival) and the equal-weighted NRR (mean_i m_i, the typical account's retention) both sit well below 100%. The headline is a concentration artifact: it measures whose DOLLARS you kept, not whether CUSTOMERS stayed, and the two diverge exactly when revenue is concentrated.

## The folk belief
"NRR above 100% is the gold-standard retention signal — negative net churn means we keep and grow our customers, so the base is healthy and we can pour acquisition on top." Operators read a single blended NRR as a customer-retention grade, celebrate crossing 100%, and under-invest in the small-account tail that is quietly bleeding logos — because the tail's churned dollars are invisible next to a couple of expanding whales. They also treat NRR and logo/customer retention as the same thing pointing the same way, when a concentrated book makes them diverge sharply and even flip sign.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
Take a book of accounts, each with starting recurring revenue r_i and a retained-revenue multiple this period m_i (m_i=0 if the account churned; m_i = the expansion factor if it survived and grew/held). Three retention numbers you can compute from the SAME accounts:

  NRR_dollar = Σ r_i·m_i / Σ r_i   = Σ w_i·m_i,  w_i = r_i/Σr   (dollar-weighted)
  logo_ret   = (# survived) / N                                   (count-weighted survival)
  NRR_equal  = (1/N) Σ m_i                                        (equal-weighted multiple)

NRR_dollar is a **revenue-share-weighted mean of the retention multiple**. Its value depends not just on the m_i but on their CORRELATION with the weights w_i. Model two segments — a fraction f of logos are whales (revenue R_big, survival p_w, expansion e_w) and the rest minnows (revenue R_small≪R_big, survival p_m<p_w, expansion e_m≈1). Then the whales own a revenue share

  w_w = f·R_big / (f·R_big + (1−f)·R_small),

which is near 1 whenever R_big≫R_small even at small f (10% of logos at 100× revenue = 91.7% of dollars). So:

  NRR_dollar = w_w·E[m_w] + (1−w_w)·E[m_m] ≈ E[m_w]   (whale-dominated),
  logo_ret   = f·p_w + (1−f)·p_m           ≈ p_m       (minnow-dominated, most logos are minnows),
  NRR_equal  = f·E[m_w] + (1−f)·E[m_m]     ≈ E[m_m]    (minnow-dominated too),

with E[m_w]=p_w·e_w, E[m_m]=p_m·e_m. Consequences the "NRR>100% = healthy" instinct misses:

1. **NRR and logo retention DIVERGE — NRR overstates customer retention.** NRR_dollar≈E[m_w] can sit far above logo_ret≈p_m. A dollar-NRR of 118% coexists with 63.5% logo retention: a third of customers gone while the headline says negative net churn. "Negative net churn" is silent on how many customers stayed.

2. **The >100% is the WEIGHTING, not the retention.** NRR_equal — the same accounts with the revenue weights REMOVED — is ≈E[m_m]<1: strip concentration and the book is contracting per logo. The headline was manufactured by a few whales' dollars; it flips below 100% the moment every account counts equally.

3. **The gap IS revenue concentration.** NRR_dollar − NRR_equal is a function of how much revenue sits in the high-multiple accounts. A wide NRR-vs-logo (or NRR-vs-equal) gap is a read on concentration risk, not retention health — you are one whale-departure away from the headline collapsing.

On the pinned world (f=0.10, R_big=100, R_small=1, p_w=0.95, e_w=1.30, p_m=0.60, e_m=1.00) the whales own w_w=0.9174 of revenue, so NRR_dollar=0.9174·1.235+0.0826·0.60=1.1826 (118% — top-decile-looking), logo_ret=0.10·0.95+0.90·0.60=0.635, and NRR_equal=0.10·1.235+0.90·0.60=0.6635 (below 100% — contracting). The 118% is pure concentration.

## Why it matters (venture-ops)
NRR (net revenue / net dollar retention) is THE board-slide SaaS health metric, and crossing 100% is read as customer-retention excellence. This proposal gives the operator a hard composition identity: NRR is a revenue-share-weighted mean of the retained multiple, so it reports whose DOLLARS you kept, not whether CUSTOMERS stayed, and the two diverge whenever revenue is concentrated. Concretely here (10% of logos at 100× revenue): dollar-NRR reads 118% while 36.5% of logos churned and the equal-weighted NRR is 66% — the same book is contracting per customer. Decision rules: (1) never report NRR alone — pair it with logo (count-weighted) retention and an equal-weighted NRR, and read the GAP as a concentration measure; (2) treat NRR>100% on a concentrated book as fragility, not strength — the headline lives or dies on a handful of whale renewals; (3) segment retention by revenue band before celebrating — the bleeding small-account tail is invisible in the blended number; (4) drive expansion/retention where the LOGOS are (the tail), not only where the dollars are, if the goal is a durable base rather than a whale-dependent one. The trap is reading a concentration artifact as customer-retention health and starving the churning base that the metric structurally cannot see.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The composition identity (NRR = Σw_i·m_i, w_i=r_i/Σr — NRR is a revenue-share-weighted mean of the retained multiple) is model-FREE: it is just the definition of net revenue retention rewritten as a weighted average, so ANY book whose revenue weights w_i correlate POSITIVELY with the retained multiple m_i has NRR_dollar > NRR_equal, and the divergence from logo retention follows whenever the high-revenue accounts have the higher survival/expansion. What is model-dependent is the SIZE of the effect (hence whether the gates clear a given margin): the pinned world uses a clean two-segment book (a fixed whale fraction at a fixed revenue ratio, per-account Bernoulli survival, a fixed expansion factor). A different revenue distribution (continuous Pareto/lognormal), a weaker size↔retention correlation, or whales that do not expand changes the exact NRR values but not the qualitative facts (NRR_dollar a revenue-weighted mean; NRR_dollar>NRR_equal whenever big accounts retain better; NRR silent on logo counts) whenever revenue is concentrated and correlated with retention — the standard heavy-tailed SaaS book. Assumptions: (a) whales retain and expand better than minnows (the empirically usual size↔retention correlation; if it were REVERSED, NRR would understate retention — the direction is what the size correlation sets, and the honest scope is the usual positive correlation); (b) revenue is concentrated (R_big≫R_small at small f — remove concentration and the mirage shrinks toward zero, so the claim is about concentrated books, exactly where NRR is quoted); (c) a single period, no cohort/tenure dynamics (this is a cross-sectional metric-composition fact, deliberately orthogonal to P106's tenure survivorship); (d) m_i=0 on churn, expansion factor on survival (the standard net-retention accounting). The claim is scoped: on a concentrated book whose large accounts retain and expand better than a churning small-account tail — the standard SaaS description — dollar-weighted NRR reads above 100% while logo and equal-weighted retention sit below it, demonstrated on the pinned constants and mechanism-explained via the revenue-share-weighted mean (Simpson's paradox), not asserted as a universal law. The direction (NRR_dollar>NRR_equal, NRR_dollar overstates logo retention) holds for any concentrated book with a positive size↔retention correlation; the pinned f/R/p/e are illustrative and bracket the mirage.
