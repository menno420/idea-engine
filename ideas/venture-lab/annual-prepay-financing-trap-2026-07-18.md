## The phenomenon (one line)
Billing a 12-month subscription monthly gives the firm 1 unit of revenue at the start of each month; offering an annual-prepay discount d gives it 12(1−d) once, at t=0. Moving a customer to annual prepay therefore SELLS a 12-month revenue stream for a discounted lump sum — the firm borrows ~11 months of revenue from its customer now and "repays" by forgoing the discount d·12. The implied monthly financing rate is the IRR r* of that incremental cash flow (Σ_{t=0}^{11}(1+r*)^{−t}=12(1−d)); the implied annual cost is APR_implied=(1+r*)^12−1. For a modest-looking "2 months free" (d=1/6) that APR is ~51% — the firm is borrowing at ~51% a year, and at any realistic cost of capital the discounted deal is worth strictly less in present value than simply billing monthly.

## The folk belief
"Annual prepay is free cash: we give the customer a small discount and in exchange we collect a full year up front instead of dribbling it out monthly — great for cash flow and it locks them in. The discount is a minor cost for a big cash-timing win." Operators book the annual plan as `cash_now = 12·price·(1−d)` and treat the discount as a small marketing expense, missing that the earlier cash is not free — it is borrowed against future revenue at a computable rate, and that rate (the IRR of the timing swap) is enormous: a 16.7% discount implies an ~51% APR. They also conflate the two real benefits (earlier cash + churn lock-in) with the discount's cost, and never compute the fair break-even discount their own cost of capital justifies.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
Normalize the monthly subscription price to 1. Consider a 12-month contract under two billing schemes, evaluated at the firm's monthly cost of capital (opportunity/discount rate) r:

**Monthly billing.** The firm collects 1 at the start of each month t = 0,1,…,11. Present value (an annuity-due of 12 payments):
S(r) = Σ_{t=0}^{11} (1+r)^{−t} = (1 − (1+r)^{−12}) / (1 − (1+r)^{−1}).

**Annual prepay at discount d.** The firm collects 12(1−d) once, at t=0. Present value = 12(1−d) (already at t=0, undiscounted).

**The incremental cash flow (annual minus monthly).** Switching a customer from monthly to annual prepay changes the firm's receipts by: +[12(1−d) − 1] at t=0 (it gets the whole discounted year now instead of just month-0's payment) and −1 at each t = 1,…,11 (those months are already collected). This stream has exactly ONE sign change (a big positive inflow now, then eleven outflows) — by Descartes/Norström the IRR is unique. The break-even monthly rate r* is the IRR:
12(1−d) − 1 − Σ_{t=1}^{11}(1+r*)^{−t} = 0  ⇔  Σ_{t=0}^{11}(1+r*)^{−t} = 12(1−d)  ⇔  S(r*) = 12(1−d).
The implied ANNUAL financing cost is APR_implied = (1+r*)^12 − 1.

Consequences the "free cash" instinct misses:

1. **The implied financing APR is huge.** S(r) is strictly decreasing in r from S(0)=12; solving S(r*)=12(1−1/6)=10 gives r*≈0.035032/month, so APR_implied=(1.035032)^12−1≈**0.5116** (51.16%). A "2 months free" annual offer is economically identical to borrowing ~11 months of revenue at a ~51% annual rate. Unless the firm's true cost of capital exceeds ~51%/yr, it is overpaying for the cash.

2. **At a realistic cost of capital the discount DESTROYS NPV.** With a real monthly cost of capital r_true=0.01 (≈12.68% APR — a healthy company's opportunity rate), monthly billing is worth S(0.01)=**11.367628** months of present-value revenue, while the annual-prepay deal is worth only 12(1−d)=**10.0**. Moving a customer to annual prepay forfeits **1.368** months of revenue in NPV — **11.40%** of the 12-month contract value — because the discount (2 months) vastly exceeds the time-value of pulling the remaining cash forward at 1%/mo. This is a deadweight loss to the firm, not a redistribution.

3. **The fair break-even discount is tiny.** The most the firm should give at cost of capital r_true is d* = 1 − S(r_true)/12 = 1 − 11.367628/12 = **0.052698** (~5.27%, ~0.63 months) — the discount at which annual prepay is NPV-neutral. The market-norm "2 months free" (16.67%) is **3.16×** the fair discount. Every point of discount beyond d* is a dollar of NPV handed away for cash the firm could raise far more cheaply.

On the pinned world (monthly price=1, d=1/6, r_true=1%/mo): APR_implied=**51.16%** vs real APR **12.68%**; monthly-billing PV **11.37** vs annual-prepay PV **10.0** (−11.40% of contract value); fair discount **5.27%** vs offered **16.67%**. The prepay discount is expensive debt, not free cash.

## Why it matters (venture-ops)
Annual-prepay pricing is a first-order subscription/SaaS decision — "2 months free on annual" is a near-universal default — and the folk model books it as "free cash + a small discount." This proposal gives the operator a hard cash-flow identity: an annual-prepay discount is borrowing against future revenue at an implied APR equal to the IRR of the timing swap, and for the market-norm 16.7% discount that APR is ~51% — four times a healthy firm's real cost of capital. Concretely here: "2 months free" is economically borrowing ~11 months of revenue at ~51%/yr, it destroys ~11.4% of contract value in NPV at a 1%/mo cost of capital, and the fair break-even discount is only ~5.3% — the market norm over-discounts by ~3.2×. Decision rules: (1) price every prepay discount as debt — compute its implied APR (the IRR of the incremental cash flow) and refuse it whenever that exceeds your true cost of capital; (2) cap the discount at the FAIR break-even d* = 1 − S(r_true)/12 that your own cost of capital justifies (here ~5.3%, ~0.63 months, not 2 months); (3) only exceed d* when you are genuinely capital-constrained ABOVE the implied rate (you literally cannot raise money more cheaply and the alternative is dilution or missing payroll) OR when the annual lock-in buys enough measurable CHURN reduction to clear the implied-APR bar — quantify that retention benefit separately and require it to pay for the discount, don't assume it; (4) never justify a prepay discount by "cash flow" alone — cheap-capital firms should push MONTHLY billing and invest the balance-sheet room elsewhere. The trap is booking annual prepay as free money and missing that the discount is an implied ~51% loan the firm took from its own customers.

## Model basis (declared model-dependence — the P024 discipline)
The core result is model-robust for ANY subscription where the customer pays a level amount per period and the firm discounts future cash at a positive rate: pulling N periods of revenue forward for a discounted lump sum is borrowing, and its implied rate is the IRR of the incremental stream — a definitional identity, not an assumption. The direction (a nominal prepay discount above the fair break-even d*=1−S(r_true)/N destroys NPV, and the market-norm discount implies an APR far above a healthy firm's cost of capital) holds for any positive cost of capital and any N. What IS model-dependent is the exact MAGNITUDE — the 51% implied APR, the 11.4% NPV loss, the 5.3% fair discount — which is specific to the pinned constants (N=12 monthly periods, d=1/6 "2 months free", r_true=1%/mo, level price). Assumptions: (a) the firm discounts future cash at a positive opportunity/cost-of-capital rate r_true (the trap requires r_true BELOW the implied rate — a genuinely capital-starved firm with r_true > 51%/yr SHOULD take the cash, which is exactly the disclosed exception); (b) equal retention under both billing schemes (the base comparison holds the customer for the full 12 months either way — the ADDITIONAL churn-reduction / lock-in benefit of annual prepay is real and is the second legitimate reason to discount, but it is a separate, measurable quantity that must be priced on its own and is deliberately held OUT of the headline so the pure financing cost is isolated; folding a generous churn assumption in is the standard way this trap is rationalized); (c) a level per-period price, deterministic, no fees or breakage; (d) monthly billing paid at period start (annuity-due) — the same result holds for arrears billing with a slightly different S(r). The claim is scoped: for a level-price monthly subscription discounted for annual prepay under a positive cost of capital and equal retention — the standard description — the discount is implicit borrowing at the IRR of the cash-timing swap, ~51% APR for the market-norm 2-months-free, destroying ~11.4% of contract value in NPV at a 1%/mo cost of capital, with a fair break-even discount of ~5.3%; demonstrated on the pinned constants and mechanism-explained via the IRR/DCF identity, not asserted as a universal magnitude. The direction (prepay discount = expensive borrowing above cost of capital) holds for any positive-rate discounter; the 51%/11.4%/5.3% figures are the pinned illustration and bracket the effect.
