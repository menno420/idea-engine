## The phenomenon (one line)
On a quality grid Q = {1,…,1000} with uniform prior, seller reservation = q and rational buyer valuation = β·q at β = 3/2 (so β·q > q — EVERY trade strictly benefits both sides, with no transaction cost and no irrationality), the only price a competitive buyer will pay is p ≤ β·E[q | q ≤ p] = β·(p+1)/2, whose largest integer solution is p* = ⌊β/(2−β)⌋ = 3; so exactly the 3 worst qualities trade, the other 997 do not, and the destroyed fraction of mutually-beneficial gains is D = 1 − p*(p*+1)/(N(N+1)) = 1 − 12/1001000 ≈ 0.999988 — the market realizes about 0.3% of its potential volume and ~99.999% of the gains from trade evaporate.

## The folk belief
"If every possible trade is win–win, the market will function." The intuitive picture treats a market where buyers value every unit above what sellers require as obviously viable — willing buyers, willing sellers, positive surplus on every pair, so trade should clear. A reasonable observer expects at LEAST half of a wholly mutually-beneficial market to survive. The folk belief has no place for the fact that a single posted price must be paid for goods the buyer cannot tell apart, and that the sellers who accept a low price are exactly the ones holding the low-quality goods.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The trade is not priced against the average car — it is priced against the average car that is actually *offered* at that price, and offering is self-selecting on hidden quality. A seller of quality q parts with the car only if the price meets the reservation, p ≥ q, so at price p the offered set is S(p) = {q ≤ p}: the *worse* the pool, the wider it is at any given price. A rational competitive buyer, unable to observe q, will pay p only up to the expected value of what that price actually attracts, β·E[q | q ∈ S(p)]. With the uniform grid the conditional mean of {1,…,p} is (p+1)/2, so the buyer pays p iff p ≤ β·(p+1)/2.

That inequality is the whole engine. Rearranged, p(2−β) ≤ β, i.e. p ≤ β/(2−β): willingness holds on an interval starting at 0 and ending at the exact threshold, because as p rises the price (left side) grows like p while the quality it buys (right side) grows only like βp/2 < p for β < 2 — the offered pool degrades as fast as the price climbs, and faster once past the threshold. The equilibrium max trading price is therefore p* = min(N, ⌊β/(2−β)⌋), and only q ≤ p* changes hands. At β = 3/2 that is ⌊3⌋ = 3 out of 1000. The load-bearing structural fact: the collapse is driven by β’s distance below 2, not by N. p* is essentially independent of market size (⌊β/(2−β)⌋ is a constant of β alone once N exceeds it), so as the grid grows the *surviving fraction* p*/N → 0 and the destroyed gains-fraction D → 1. A larger, richer market with more mutually-beneficial trades available does not dilute the failure — it deepens it. Adverse selection needs no friction and no irrationality; the rational-expectations price, applied to an endogenously-selected pool, is the entire cause.
