## The phenomenon (one line)
You face N closed boxes; opening box i costs c_i and reveals a reward R_i from a known distribution, and you keep only the single best reward you ever uncover (outside option 0). The profit-maximizing search does NOT open boxes in order of expected reward — it opens them in decreasing order of a reservation value z_i that solves c_i = E[(R_i − z_i)^+], stops the instant the best reward in hand ≥ the largest reservation value among the still-closed boxes, and this simple index rule's expected net payoff EXACTLY equals the brute-force optimum over every possible policy (Weitzman 1979).

## The folk belief
"Search the most promising boxes first, and keep searching while a better prize is expected." The intuitive rule reads expected reward E[R_i] as the priority score — open the highest-mean box, and stop only once the best thing in hand beats the mean of everything still closed. It treats a box's opening cost as a flat toll and its mean payoff as the whole story, so it will always prefer a higher-mean box to a lower-mean one and will never walk away from a box whose expected prize still exceeds what it holds.

## The mechanism (reasoned to its fuller form — Q-0254 duty)
The priority of a box is not its expected reward; it is how much upside it buys per unit of inspection cost, and that is exactly what the reservation value prices. Define z_i by c_i = E[(R_i − z_i)^+]: z_i is the "indifference floor" at which the expected gain from the part of R_i that would beat z_i pays for exactly one opening. Because (R_i − z)^+ is convex and non-increasing in z, E[(R_i − z)^+] falls continuously from the mean (at z ≤ min support) to zero (at z = max support), so for any c_i > 0 the equation has a unique root z_i strictly below the box's maximum — and, crucially, z_i weights the RIGHT TAIL of R_i, not its center. A low-mean, high-spread box carries a fat upper tail, so its (R_i − z)^+ stays large for large z, forcing z_i HIGH; a high-mean, low-spread box has no such tail and gets a MODEST z_i. Priority tracks tail-value-per-cost, and the mean is the wrong axis.

The load-bearing structural fact is that this single index simultaneously fixes both decisions — WHICH box to open and WHEN to stop — and is provably optimal. Sort boxes by z_i descending; open them in that order; stop when the best reward held reaches the top remaining z_i. Three exactly-true consequences follow, each contradicting the folk rule: (a) you can rationally DECLINE to open a box whose expected prize exceeds every value you already hold — if its z_i is below your best-in-hand, its tail is too thin to justify the cost, so you walk away from a positive-mean gap; (b) a LOWER-MEAN, HIGHER-SPREAD box is opened FIRST when its tail earns it the higher z_i; (c) the index rule leaves NOTHING on the table — its expected value equals the full dynamic-programming optimum over all 2^N-ordered policies exactly. The folk rule silently assumes priority = mean and stopping = mean-comparison; Weitzman's rule prices the tail against the cost, and search theory says: order by reservation value, and sometimes skip the box you most expect to win.
