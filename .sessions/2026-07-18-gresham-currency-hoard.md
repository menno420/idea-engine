## Objective

Draft and land round-34 GAME-slot PROPOSAL 147 — Gresham currency hoarding. Folk belief: "issue a premium HARD currency at par with the soft currency and it will circulate and drive the economy — two interchangeable currencies at parity spend equally." Counterintuitive claim: under Gresham's law (bad money drives out good), rational players spend the currency whose purchasing power DEPRECIATES each period (the "melting ice cube") and HOARD the STABLE currency, so the good currency's circulation velocity COLLAPSES toward zero while the bad currency does almost all the settlement — even though both faucets mint symmetric nominal income. The SIGN of the circulation gap (share settled in bad − share settled in good) depends on spender rationality: with naive proportional spenders there is NO Gresham gap (good actually circulates MORE, tracking its larger real holdings), with rational hoarders the gap is strongly positive (good driven out of circulation). ## 💡 Session idea

The R=20 → R=40 tune this slice made to widen the G1 estimator-agreement margin (z=−2.09 → z=−0.70) is a recurring pattern across these paired-Monte-Carlo cards: G1 is a two-half split whose noise scales as ~1/√(R/2), so a headline that is genuinely stable can still throw a borderline G1 |z| near 3 purely from too-few trials, tempting a constant-fiddle that masks real instability. Candidate follow-up: a "G1 headroom" convention for every paired-estimator proposal — pick R so the expected half-vs-half |z| sits comfortably below ~1.5 (not just under the 3σ bar), and record the R-sensitivity of G1 in the card, so a reviewer can tell an honestly-stable estimator (G1 tightens smoothly as R grows) from one propped up at a single lucky R. Pairs with the P143 "baseline-degeneracy audit" (is the control arm a live baseline?) and the P024 model-dependence discipline (does the sign depend on the model?): this one asks "is the estimator's stability real, or a low-R artifact of the split?".
