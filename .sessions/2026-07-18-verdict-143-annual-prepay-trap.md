# VERDICT 143 mirror — the annual-prepay financing trap / subscription cash-flow economics: the folk model of an annual-prepay discount is that it is free money — "give the customer X% off to pay a year up front and we get 12 months of cash now instead of dribbled out monthly, great for cash flow." It is NOT free. Selling a 12-month revenue stream for a discounted lump sum is a LOAN the firm takes FROM its customer, and the discount is the interest. Relative to billing monthly, the firm receives 12·(1−d) at t=0 instead of 1 per month for 12 months — it has borrowed ~11 months of revenue and repays by forgoing the discount d·12; the implied financing rate is the internal rate of return of that incremental cash flow, the monthly r* solving S(r*)=Σ_{t=0}^{11}(1+r*)^{−t}=12·(1−d), with implied annual cost APR_implied=(1+r*)^12−1. Two facts the instinct misses: (1) the implied financing APR is HUGE — a modest-looking "2 months free" (d=1/6≈16.67%) implies r*≈3.5%/mo so APR_implied≈51%, the firm borrowing ~11 months of its own future revenue at ~51%/yr; (2) at any realistic cost of capital the discounted deal DESTROYS NPV — with r_true=1%/mo (≈12.68% APR) monthly billing is worth S(0.01)=11.367628 months of present-value revenue vs the annual-prepay deal's 12·(1−d)=10.0, a loss of ~1.37 months (≈11.40% of contract value), and the FAIR break-even discount is only d*=1−S(r_true)/12=0.052698 (≈5.27%, ~0.63 months) so the market-norm "2 months free" over-discounts by 3.162697×.
