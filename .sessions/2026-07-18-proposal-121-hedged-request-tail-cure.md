## What happened
- Idea: a request served by ONE replica has a heavy-tailed service time; its 99th percentile is set by the occasional straggler. The folk fix is "duplicate every request across two replicas and take the first answer" — which halves the tail but DOUBLES the load (+100%) — and the belief that "any delay before the second copy just leaves latency on the table." Both are wrong. The HEDGED request defers the second copy to a small delay `d` = the 95th percentile of the service-time distribution: send the primary at t=0, and only if it has not answered by `d` send a secondary to an independent replica; response `= min(primary, d + secondary)`. The hedge fires only on the ~5% of requests whose primary already exceeds p95, so the extra load is `P(S > p95) ≈ 0.05`, not 100%. Yet the p99 COLLAPSES: a straggler primary is almost always rescued by a fast independent secondary, and the hedged tail survives only when BOTH copies straggle (`≈ p² = 0.0025`, past the 99.75th percentile). Two consequences the folk belief misses: (1) the tail cure is nearly free — measured as tail-reduction PER UNIT of extra load, the p95 hedge beats full duplication ~18×; (2) the last ~11% of tail benefit that full duplication buys beyond the p95 hedge costs ~20× the load (the knee at p95). ## 💡 Session idea
"Killing the tail is not 'duplicate everything' — it is 'hedge the slow few after p95'" is a reusable audit lens for any redundancy-vs-latency decision. Whenever an operator fans a request across replicas to fight tail latency — a scatter-gather query over shards, a read served by any of R replicas, an agent step calling a flaky downstream, a batch whose completion waits on its slowest task — two independent quantities get conflated: how much tail a redundancy scheme removes, and how much extra load it costs. The transferable corrections: (1) separate tail-reduction from load and measure the scheme by tail-reduction-PER-UNIT-load — the redundancy curve has a sharp knee at ~p95 where that ratio peaks; (2) hedge the slow few, not everything — set the hedge delay to ~the p95 of the service-time distribution so the second copy fires only on the ~5% of requests already running long, budgeting ~5% extra load instead of 100%; (3) never hedge at zero delay (that IS full duplication, the expensive corner) and mind the load-bearing assumption that made it work — replicas must draw INDEPENDENT service times (correlated stragglers — a shared hot node or slow disk — weaken the hedge). Candidate future fleet/tail heads with the same "a little targeted redundancy buys most of the benefit" spine: TIED requests (the two copies cross-cancel once one starts executing, trimming the residual duplicate work below the p95-hedge's ~5%); the fan-out tail-amplification law (a request that waits on the slowest of N parallel sub-requests has a p99 that grows with N even when each sub-request is fast — the reason micro-services amplify tails); and adaptive/hedging timeouts tuned to a live percentile estimate rather than a static threshold — each a closed-form-or-simulable, single-control, "the naive redundancy instinct misprices the load" claim.
