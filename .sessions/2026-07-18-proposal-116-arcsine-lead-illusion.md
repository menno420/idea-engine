## 💡 Session idea
"A perfectly fair process is fair in its MEAN, not level in its PATH" is a reusable audit lens for any close, driftless, back-and-forth signal read as evidence of an edge. Whenever a near-parity contest exposes a "who has been ahead / for how long" statistic — a tied competitive match, a near-parity A/B test, two comparable trading strategies or similar-skill agents, a cumulative-score race, a running vote or win-margin — the fraction of time on the leading side is arcsine-distributed (U-shaped: one side leads almost the whole run with high probability, a genuine see-saw is the RAREST outcome) even at ZERO edge, because a driftless walk is null-recurrent and its zero-crossings are rare, so long one-sided excursions dominate. The transferable correction: never infer an edge from lead DURATION or lopsidedness in a close-to-fair contest — that duration concentrates at the extremes by construction; grade the edge from the DRIFT (net margin per step / terminal gap ± its standard error), read a long one-sided lead as the SIGNATURE of fairness rather than superiority, and budget stopping rules and confidence intervals to the arcsine tails rather than to a naive "it should hover near 50/50." Candidate future unrelated/probability heads with the same "distribution ≠ mean" spine: the second arcsine law (the time of the LAST lead-change / maximum is also arcsine, so the max tends to occur near the very start or very end), the ballot problem / cycle lemma (the probability one side leads THROUGHOUT is (a−b)/(a+b)), and the reflection-principle running-maximum law E[max]∝√n — each a closed-form-anchored, single-control, "the path is not the average" claim.
