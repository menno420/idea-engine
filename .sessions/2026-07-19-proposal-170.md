## GROUNDING (verified at HEAD)
Winner's curse (Capen, Clapp & Campbell 1971; common-value auction theory): when N bidders form unbiased but noisy estimates of a common value and the highest bid wins, the winner's estimate is the maximum order statistic and thus biased high — the winner systematically overpays. Applied to venture: a hot round with many competing term sheets is a common-value auction on the company's intrinsic value.

## Pre-registered gates (match the shipped verifier), z_gate = 3.0
- **G1** — winner overpays and the overpayment deepens with N: with gaussian valuation error, mean winner excess at N=8 is negative with |z| ≥ 3, and is more negative than at N=2 with |z| ≥ 3 on the difference.
- **G2** — conditional-on-winning reversal: a single arbitrary bidder's excess is unbiased (mean ≈ 0), but conditioning on winning selects overpayment — winner excess is below the unconditional mean with |z| ≥ 3.
- **G3** — robustness under a heavier-tailed, shifted error distribution (Laplace, inflated dispersion): winner still overpays at N=8 and the overpayment still deepens with N, each |z| ≥ 3.
- all_pass = G1 ∧ G2 ∧ G3.

## Probe questions
**1. Is the curse just an artifact of naive bidding rather than a real venture phenomenon?** No — the head models the documented naive/unshaded bid; the claim is precisely that winning selects overpayment, which is the winner's curse, and disciplined Bayesian shading is the known correction.
**2. Could the ≥3σ gates pass trivially because TRIALS is huge?** The gates test sign and monotonic direction, not just significance; a null world with no selection effect leaves winner excess at zero regardless of TRIALS.
**3. Does the result survive a non-gaussian error world?** G3 answers this under a heavier-tailed, higher-dispersion Laplace distribution.
