## What happened
- Idea: an annual-prepay discount is usually read as free cash — "give a small discount, collect a full year up front instead of dribbling it out monthly; great for cash flow and it locks the customer in." Reasoned to its fuller form (Q-0254): the earlier cash is NOT free — moving a customer from monthly billing to an annual-prepay lump sum SELLS a 12-month revenue stream for a discounted amount, i.e. the firm BORROWS ~11 months of revenue against its own future and "repays" by forgoing the discount d·12. Normalize the monthly price to 1: monthly billing has present value `S(r)=Σ_{t=0}^{11}(1+r)^{−t}` (an annuity-due at monthly cost of capital r); annual prepay at discount d has present value `12(1−d)` (all at t=0). The incremental (monthly→annual-prepay) cash flow is `+[12(1−d)−1]` at t=0 and `−1` at t=1..11 — a single sign change, so by Descartes/Norström the IRR is unique, and the break-even monthly rate r* is that IRR: `S(r*)=12(1−d)`, with implied annual financing cost `APR_implied=(1+r*)^12−1`.
- Two facts the "free cash" instinct misses: (1) the implied financing APR is huge — for a modest-looking "2 months free" (d=1/6) `r*≈0.035032/mo` so `APR_implied≈0.5116` (~51%), i.e. borrowing ~11 months of revenue at ~51%/yr; (2) at a realistic cost of capital the discount DESTROYS NPV — with r_true=1%/mo (≈12.68% APR) monthly billing is worth `S(0.01)=11.367628` months of present-value revenue vs the annual deal's `12(1−d)=10.0`, a loss of 1.368 months (~11.40% of contract value), and the FAIR break-even discount is only `d*=1−S(r_true)/12=0.052698` (~5.27%), so the market norm over-discounts by 3.162697×. The discount is expensive debt, worth paying only when the firm is genuinely capital-constrained above the implied rate or when the annual lock-in buys enough MEASURABLE churn reduction to clear that bar (both held OUT of the headline so the pure financing cost is isolated).

## 💡 Session idea
"Any nominal discount that moves cash across time is a hidden interest rate — before booking it as a marketing cost, price it as debt: compute the IRR of the cash-timing swap and compare it to your real cost of capital." Whenever a decision trades a nominal price cut for earlier cash (an annual-prepay or multi-year discount, a prompt-payment / early-pay discount, factoring a receivable, a deposit-for-discount), the folk model books the discount as a small expense and the earlier cash as free — but the discount is the INTEREST on an implicit loan, and its true annual cost is the internal rate of return of the incremental cash flow (`APR_implied=(1+r*)^N−1` where `S(r*)=N(1−d)`), which for ordinary-looking discounts is startlingly high (here "2 months free" = ~51% APR). The transferable corrections: (1) never justify a prepay/early-pay discount by "cash flow" alone — compute its implied APR and refuse it whenever that exceeds your true cost of capital; (2) cap the discount at the FAIR break-even `d*=1−S(r_true)/N` your own cost of capital justifies (here ~5.3%, not the market-norm ~16.7%), and treat every point beyond d* as NPV handed away for cash you could raise more cheaply; (3) the same "a nominal number hides a rate" spine recurs across finance surfaces — a discount hides a borrowing rate (this), a late fee hides a lending rate, a lease-vs-buy spread hides an implied rate — so the diagnostic question "what interest rate does this nominal term actually imply?" generalizes far past subscription pricing. Candidate future heads on the same spine: the prompt-payment-discount (2/10 net 30) implied-APR trap, the deposit-financing implied-rate trap, and the multi-year-prepay compounding-discount trap.
