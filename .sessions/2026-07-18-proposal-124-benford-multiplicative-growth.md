## 💡 Session idea
"Random growth does NOT spread the leading digit evenly — MULTIPLICATIVE growth makes it Benford (digit 1 ~30%), ADDITIVE growth does not" is a reusable audit lens for any quantity that compounds over time. Whenever a metric is generated by repeated multiplication — prices, populations, account balances, file sizes, download/view counts, revenue that compounds — its first significant digit is Benford-distributed (`P(d)=log10(1+1/d)`, digit 1 ~30.1%), and a deviation from Benford in a compounding quantity is a genuine anomaly signal (the basis of Benford-law fraud/audit tests). The transferable corrections: (1) never expect uniform leading digits from "random" growth — multiplicativity forces Benford, additivity does not, so the digit test is really a test of the generating PROCESS; (2) the fit EMERGES with log-decades, so a Benford audit needs the quantity to span multiple orders of magnitude (a metric confined to one decade will fail Benford for benign reasons); (3) the same log-normal machinery that makes wealth concentrate (P115 Gibrat / compounding inequality) makes leading digits Benford — one mechanism, two observable signatures. Candidate future unrelated heads with the same "the generating process leaves a distributional fingerprint" spine: Zipf's law from preferential attachment (rank-frequency ~1/rank), the log-normal size distribution from multiplicative growth (Gibrat's law proper), and Benford's SECOND-digit law (P(d)=Σ_k log10(1+1/(10k+d)), flatter than the first-digit law) as a sharper audit refinement.
