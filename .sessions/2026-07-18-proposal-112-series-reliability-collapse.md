## What happened
- Idea: a SERIES system works only while ALL of its N components work, so it fails at the FIRST component failure — its lifetime is the MINIMUM of the N per-component lifetimes. For independent exponential lifetimes (rate λ, per-component MTBF T=1/λ) the minimum of N is itself exponential with the SUMMED rate Nλ (hazard rates ADD: λ_sys=∑λ_i=Nλ), so the system MTBF collapses to exactly T/N — N "individually excellent" parts give a system N× MORE failure-prone than one part, and reliability R_sys(t)=r(t)^N decays geometrically in N. The symmetric fully-parallel/redundant system (fails only at the LAST component failure, lifetime = MAXIMUM of N) has MTBF T·H_N (the N-th harmonic number), growing only LOGARITHMICALLY: at N=20 the series MTBF is T/N=0.05 while parallel is T·H_20≈3.598, so seriesing DIVIDES MTBF by 20 while 20-fold redundancy MULTIPLIES it by only ~3.6×. A single component in isolation keeps the full MTBF T, so the collapse is 100% the seriesing of many parts, not a bad part. ## 💡 Session idea
"A system that is up only while ALL of N independent parts are up has its reliability set by the PRODUCT, and its lifetime by the MINIMUM, of the parts — never the average, never the weakest read in isolation" is a reusable audit lens for any all-of-N (logical AND) dependency structure. Whenever end-to-end success requires every one of N stages/hops/links/approvals to hold — an N-microservice request path, an N-stage pipeline, a single-sourced N-link supply chain, an N-step manual checklist — the system reliability is ∏ r_i (geometric decay in N, not linear) and, for memoryless failures, the MTBF is T/N (a full division by the count), so adding a "good" part always makes the whole WORSE and the parts' individual excellence never enters except through N. The dual lever is redundancy (logical OR / maximum of lifetimes), which for the exponential buys only T·H_N — logarithmic, strongly diminishing — so it pays only on the element carrying a large share of ∑λ_i. The transferable audit: whenever reliability/availability/yield is quoted per-component and the components sit in series, MULTIPLY (never average, never anchor on the weakest), expect the MTBF to divide by the series count, and spend the budget on SHORTENING the chain or targeting redundancy at the dominant failure-rate element — not on marginally polishing an already-good part. Candidate future fleet/reliability heads: the k-out-of-n voting-system reliability sweet spot (a quorum that is neither series nor parallel), the parallel-redundancy common-cause-failure ceiling (redundancy defeated by shared shocks — the reliability analogue of P109's correlated-fleet floor), and the bathtub-curve burn-in vs wear-out provisioning tradeoff — each a "the structure, not the part quality, sets system reliability" claim with a closed-form anchor and a single-component control.
