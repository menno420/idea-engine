## What happened
- Idea: a dispatcher routes each of m tasks to the least-loaded of d workers sampled uniformly at random from n. With d=1 (pure random routing) the maximum queue length grows like ln n / ln ln n; with d=2 it collapses to ln ln n / ln 2 + O(1) — a DOUBLE-EXPONENTIAL improvement in the tail (Azar–Broder–Karlin–Upfal 1999; Mitzenmacher 2001). Every extra probe past the second only divides the already-tiny ln ln n term by ln d, so the marginal gain collapses: the entire benefit is captured at d=2, and operators who expect balance to improve smoothly with the probe count over-provision d=4..8. The trap: cap probe fan-out at d=2 and spend the saved coordination budget on load-signal freshness.
- Pinned world SEED=20260717, N_WORKERS=20000, M_TASKS=20000 (m=n balls-into-bins regime), TRIALS=120, D_VALUES=(1,2,3,4), SIGMA_GATE=3.0, PLATEAU_MAX=2.0. Verifier is a per-trial Monte-Carlo over all four d values, recording the max load; stdlib-only (random, math, json, hashlib).

## 💡 Session idea
"The whole benefit of redundancy in a random-selection process can live entirely in the FIRST unit of redundancy" is a reusable audit lens beyond load balancing. Wherever a system samples d candidates and takes the best, the d=1→2 step is often a phase change and d≥3 a vanishing constant — so the operational question is never "how many should I sample?" but "is the second sample worth its coordination cost?" (almost always yes) and "is the third?" (almost never). Candidate future fleet heads in this family: the (1+β)-choice interpolation sweet spot (a fractional second probe captures most of the d=2 win at a fraction of the coordination cost — Peres–Talwar–Wieder), the stale-load-signal ceiling (how much delay in the load reading erases the two-choices gain — the routing analogue of a control-loop lag), and the memory-vs-sampling tradeoff (one bit of remembered previous placement rivals a second probe — Mitzenmacher–Prabhakar–Shah), each a "the structure, not the probe count, sets the balance" claim with a closed-form anchor and a same-run control.
