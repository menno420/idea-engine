## What happened
- Idea: a long fleet job that can crash and restart from its last checkpoint faces two competing wastes — checkpoint OVERHEAD (~C/τ per unit useful work) and lost RECOMPUTE work on a crash (~τ/(2M) at MTBF M). Their sum C/τ + τ/(2M) is a convex falling+rising pair, so total wasted wall time is U-SHAPED in the checkpoint interval τ with a unique INTERIOR optimum τ*=√(2·C·M) (Young 1974 first-order; Daly 2006 exact) and overhead floor √(2C/M). "Checkpoint as often as you can afford" is wrong: over-checkpointing walks up the left arm of the U, wasting strictly more, exactly as wastefully in the leading order as the symmetric under-checkpointing; and the optimum scales only as √M (a 4× more reliable machine warrants only a 2× longer interval and halves the achievable overhead). The trap: set the interval to τ*=√(2·C·MTBF) and invest in shrinking the checkpoint cost C, not in raising the frequency.

## 💡 Session idea
"The safety knob has a wrong direction on BOTH ends" is a reusable audit lens beyond checkpointing. Wherever a protective action carries its own recurring cost paid unconditionally (a checkpoint, a retry, a health-probe, a redundant replica, a confirmation round-trip), the operational objective is U-shaped and the naive "more protection = safer" instinct climbs the wrong arm — so the question is never "how much protection can I afford?" but "where is the interior optimum, and how does it scale?" The Young–Daly √(2·C·M) form is one instance of a family of "balance the unconditional overhead against the conditional loss" optima; candidate future fleet heads in this family: the optimal retry/timeout budget (retry cost vs tail-latency loss, a √-type optimum against the failure rate — kin to but distinct from the metastable-retry-storm head, which is about load feedback not a single job's budget), the optimal health-probe interval (probe cost vs undetected-failure dwell time), and the optimal replication factor (replica cost vs correlated-loss probability), each a "the protection frequency is an optimisation, not a maximisation, and it scales sub-linearly in the reliability" claim with a closed-form anchor and a same-run over/under control.
