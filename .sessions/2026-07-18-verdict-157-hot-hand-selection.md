The head under verdict: in a FINITE sequence of N fair i.i.d. coin flips, select every flip that immediately follows a run of K consecutive heads and average the per-sequence fraction-of-heads across sequences; the folk belief is "a fair coin is memoryless, so the post-K-head-streak fraction of heads must be exactly 0.5, and an empirical fraction below 0.5 proves negative autocorrelation / an anti-streak (and a post-streak rate AT the base rate disproves a hot hand — the 1985 Gilovich–Vallone–Tversky conclusion)." It is NOT: take a length-N sequence of fair i.i.d. flips (heads=1, p=1/2); a position j in [K, N-1] is SELECTED iff flips j-K..j-1 are all heads (overlapping windows, the Miller–Sanjurjo convention); the per-sequence estimator = (selected flips that are heads) / (selected flips), defined iff ≥1 selected flip; average that per-sequence fraction across sequences (equal weight per sequence, conditioned on ≥1 selected flip — NOT pooled). Within one finite sequence the act of selecting a flip because its K predecessors were heads is negatively correlated with that flip being a head (a head extends the run and tends to be consumed as a predecessor of later selections; a tail ends it), so ratio-averaging across finite sequences pulls the expected fraction below 0.5 — a law-of-small-numbers effect that vanishes as N→∞, grows with K, shrinks with N, and has an exact closed form.
